4. What is overexertion or overtraining? What are the safety protocols​


Overtraining

When a person exercises more than their body can recuperate from intense activity, overtraining results. When a person is overtrained, their performance may plateau or decline as a result of their inability to maintain a certain level of performance or training load, which exceeds their capacity for recuperation. Overtrained individuals stop improving and may even start to lose fitness and strength. In athletics, overtraining is sometimes referred to as chronic weariness, burnout, and overstress.

Further explanation

Advice on How to Prevent Overtraining Injuries

Avoid escalating the level of workout difficulty too rapidly.

Progression must be gradual. You may prevent injuries by making and adhering to a plan that organizes your workout toward your goals.

Slow down and control your expectations.

In other words, start off slowly. When you start doing intense exercise after being inactive for a while or try to reach your fitness goal rapidly, overtraining injuries can occur quickly.

Be aware of your body.

You can be overdoing it if you have persistent soreness or pain, or if a particular exercise puts strain on your body. Overtraining is an indication of fatigue as well.

Take rest periods.

Not arranging rest days is a typical sign of overtraining. Restorative days are necessary for the continued health of bones, joints, and muscles.

Feed your body nourishing nutrients.

Make sure your diet contains enough proteins, grains, veggies, fruits, and calcium-rich foods.

Rest.

Sleep is crucial to our body's capacity for growth and repair.

The following are some common results of

overexertion and how to treat them

1. Chronic pain - Consult a doctor if your pain is too acute. It’s likely the doctor will recommend rest, proper nutrition and a gradual transition to normal activities by starting out slow with only a few light activities. If your pain is too much to bear then you might also need over-the-counter medications and physiotherapy.

2. Injuries - These injuries are best treated by a professional, so consult a doctor as soon as possible. Joint and muscle injuries are usually treated by resting the concerned areas, wearing a brace to reaffirm its stability and doing light stretches guided by a trained physiotherapist. Applying hot or cold compresses and taking over-the-counter medications can help you deal with the pain of these injuries.

3. Cardiovascular events - Consulting a doctor and adhering to their guidance and prescriptions is vital in these cases. You might be given medications, recommended changes in your lifestyle and referred to a physiotherapist to restart physical activity as per your individual needs and pace of recovery.

4. Low immune responses - chronic overexertion can cripple your immune responses and make you more susceptible to illnesses, especially infections and wounds or injuries that take too long to heal. The best way to deal with this decline in immunity is take ample rest and give your immune system a good boost. You could do this by consulting a doctor and a nutritionist who can recommend a nutrient-dense balanced diet and any dietary supplements you may need.
