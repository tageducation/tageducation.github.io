It is especially widespread among the East Asian elites. It includes ancient Chinese, ancient philosophy, ancient music, clothing, architecture, calligraphy, painting, Go, tea art, drink art, Cooking art, poetry, flower arrangement, antiques.

Chinese elegant culture originated in China. The original China refers to Luoyang. Luoyang is located in the geographic center of East Asia and where King Zhou lived, so it is called the Central Kingdom. Luoyang is very developed, far ahead of other regions by a thousand years. They invented ancient Chinese, compiled the first philosophy book I Ching, opened the first Confucian school, the first Taoist temple, and the first Buddhist temple. They invented silk, and obeyed the etiquette system. They call themselves Hua, which means people in gorgeous clothes.

At this time, other regions were still primitive society, with no writing, no clothing, and no system. Everyone wants to learn advanced civilization. Many people misunderstand that it is the China-centered theory put forward by Chinese nationalists, thinking that they are civilized people and others are barbarians. The real history is that ethnic minorities themselves put forward the "Huayi" theory. King Zhou are also a barbarians . They don't want to be barbarians. They think that barbarians can become civilized people by absorbing Chinese culture. Therefore, Chinese culture is not exclusive to a certain nation from the beginning, It gradually developed into the mainstream culture of East Asia, and people still like it today.

After Qin unified China. China is no longer a geographical term, but an imperial term. More and more ethnic minorities join China, which also makes Chinese culture more diverse and complex. In addition to elegant culture, there are folk culture and ethnic minority culture. Qin established an imperial system, and all dynasties including modern China followed the dynasty system, which also made China different from all countries. So many people think that China retains more of Qin and Han culture.

China and Japan are separated by the sea, so there are only high-level exchanges between China and Japan, not people-to-people exchanges. Japan mainly absorbs elegant culture. And because Japan is an isolated island, many historical sites have been protected, so people think that Japan retains more of the Tang Dynasty culture. But Japan has never absorbed Chinese philosophy, but has its own philosophical system of worship power and beauty. "Sword and Chrysanthemum" illustrates this culture well.

Due to its closer geographical location, South Korea has absorbed more Chinese culture, especially the culture and system of the Ming Dynasty. But they also absorbed a lot of Japanese culture and Western culture, so it is a mixed culture. This is why K-POP is liked by everyone.

Differences- When it comes to gestures, bowing is one aspect of each culture that most assume is the same, but in fact, it has evolved in each country over the years. In Japan and Korea, a slight bow when greeting each other and a deeper bow in more formal situations are still considered appropriate.

However, in China, the handshake has actually become a common greeting, with only a slight head nod rather than the traditional bow.

I noticed this a little in my experiences with Chinese people, but especially with the Korean and Japanese. Even in my Skype lessons with the latter, we often end the call with a bow out of respect, which is definitely unique from my other students.

Another mannerism that I noticed in everyday life was the volume and tone of speaking. I visited Hokkaido in northern Japan on vacation once and began to see and hear Chinese tourists from a mile away each time I got on a train. Upon entering a train or other public transportation, Japanese and Koreans typically remain eerily silent and even keep their laughter to a minimum. Chinese people, on the other hand, don’t seem to have the cultural custom of quietness in public spaces.

So, you’ll often see people in China laughing and raising their voices, which is a stark contrast to Japan and Korea. I’m sure this has something to do with their long history of such held traditions, but that would take an entire course in Asian history, so I digress.
