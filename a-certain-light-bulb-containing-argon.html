5. There are 3 defective light bulbs in a bin containing 30 light bulbs. How many ways can you select at least 2 defective light bulbs when you choose 4 light bulbs at random?​


Answer:

We need to find the number of ways we can select at least 2 defective light bulbs out of the 4 light bulbs we choose. To do this, we can use the complement principle, which states that the number of ways to do something is equal to the total number of ways it could be done minus the number of ways it cannot be done.

There are 30 light bulbs in the bin, out of which 3 are defective, so there are 27 non-defective light bulbs. To choose 4 light bulbs at random, we have 30 total choices, out of which 27 are non-defective.

There are 30C4 = 30!/(4!26!) = 30292827/432*1 = 30,855 ways to select 4 light bulbs at random.

Out of these 30,855 ways, we need to find the number of ways that we can select less than 2 defective light bulbs.

There are 3C0 = 1 ways to select 0 defective light bulbs.

There are 3C1 = 3 ways to select 1 defective light bulbs.

[So in total there are 1+3 = 4 ways to select less than 2 defective light bulbs.]

Therefore, the number of ways to select at least 2 defective light bulbs is 30,855 - 4 = 30,851.

11. why are light bulbs filled with argon gas rather than oxygen gas​


Answer:

Argon gas is used in fluorescent and incandescent light bulbs to stop the oxygen in the light bulbs from corroding the hot tungsten filament.The use of argon in light bulbs prevents the evaporation of the tungsten filaments, which results in increased light bulb life.

Argon is also used to create an inert atmosphere for growing semiconductor crystals, arc welding and for processes that need protection from other kinds of atmospheric gases. Argon was originally discovered in 1894 by an English chemist named Lord Rayleigh and a Scottish chemist named Sir William Ramsay. Argon is a nonmetal gas at room temperature and is assigned the number 18 on the period table of elements.

12. A certain light bulb containing argon has a pressure of 1.20 atm at 18°C. If it will be heated to 85°C at constant volume, what will be the resulting pressure? Is it enough to cause sudden breakage of the bulb?​​


GAY LUSSAC'S LAW

===========================================

[tex]\large\underline{\sf{Problem:}}[/tex]

A certain light bulb containing argon has a pressure of 1.20 atm at 18°C. If it will be heated to 85°C at constant volume, what will be the resulting pressure? Is it enough to cause sudden breakage of the bulb?

===========================================

[tex]\large\underline{\sf{Answer:}}[/tex]

[tex] \therefore[/tex] The resulting pressure is 1.48 atm and the resulting pressure is enough to cause sudden breakage of the bulb.

===========================================

[tex]\large\underline{\sf{Solution:}}[/tex]

Gay Lussac's Law

[tex]\qquad \qquad \sf \implies\boxed{ \bold{ \: \: \dfrac{\green{P_1}}{\purple{T_1}}=\dfrac{\red{P_2}}{\blue{T_2}} \: \: }} \: \: \bigstar \\[/tex]

where :

[tex]\qquad \sf \implies \: \green{P_1} = \green{Initial \: Pressure}[/tex]

[tex]\qquad \sf \implies \: \purple{T_1} = \purple{Initial \: Temperature}[/tex]

[tex]\qquad \sf \implies \: \red{P_2} = \red{Final \: Pressure}[/tex]

[tex]\qquad \sf \implies \: \blue{T_2} = \blue{Final \: Temperature} \\ [/tex]

Temperature must be in kelvins (K).

To convert Celsius to kelvins, add 273.15.

Given values :

[tex]\qquad \qquad \sf \implies \green{P_1} = \green{1.20 \: atm}[/tex]

[tex]\qquad \qquad \sf \implies \purple{T_1} = \purple{18^{\circ}C} = \purple{18} + \orange{273.15} = \purple{291 K}[/tex]

[tex]\qquad \qquad \sf \implies \blue{T_2} = \blue{85^{\circ} C} = \blue{85} + \orange{273.15} = \blue{358 \: K} \\ [/tex]

Substitute the given values into the equation and solve for P_2 :

[tex]\qquad\implies \sf \dfrac{ \green{P_1}}{ \purple{T_1}}=\dfrac{ \red{P_2}}{ \blue{T_2}} \\[/tex]

[tex]\qquad\implies \sf \dfrac{ \green{1.20\:atm}}{ \purple{291\:K}}=\dfrac{ \red{P_2}}{ \blue{358\:K}} \\[/tex]

[tex]\qquad\sf \implies \sf \red{P_2}=\dfrac{ \green{1.20\:atm} \cdot \blue{358\:K}}{ \purple{291\:K}} \\[/tex]

[tex]\qquad\sf \implies \sf \red{P_2}=\dfrac{ \green{429.6\:atm}}{ \purple{291\:K}} \\ [/tex]

[tex]\qquad\sf \implies \red{P_2}= \red{ \pmb{1.48\:atm }}\\ [/tex]

[tex] \therefore[/tex] The resulting pressure is 1.48 atm and the resulting pressure is enough to cause sudden breakage of the bulb.

===========================================

25. A certain light bulb containing argon has pressure of 1.20 atm at 18°C. If it will be heated to 85°C at constant volume, what will be the resulting pressure? Is it enough to cause sudden breakage of the bulb?​


Answer:

[tex]\therefore[/tex] The resulting pressure of the light bulb is 1.47 atm

Explanation:

To know the resulting pressure of the light bulb, we have to use derived Gay-Lussac Equation.

[tex]\boxed{\bold{P_2 = \frac{P_1T_2}{T_1}}}[/tex]

Given that:

[tex]P_1[/tex] = 1.20 atm[tex]T_1[/tex] = 18°C + 273.15°K = 291°K [tex]T_2[/tex] = 85°C + 273.15°K = 358°K

Now, substitute the given values to the formula:

[tex]\rm{P_2 = \frac{(1.20)(358)}{291} = \frac{429.6}{291} = 1.47 \ atm}[/tex]  

[tex]\therefore[/tex] The resulting pressure of the light bulb is 1.47 atm
