9. what do you think is the impact of hofstede's dimension of cultural variability?​


Answer:

Hofstede's dimensions of cultural variability are a set of cultural dimensions that were developed by Dutch social psychologist Geert Hofstede. These dimensions are used to describe and compare the values, beliefs, and behaviors of different cultures. The dimensions are:

-Individualism vs. collectivism: This dimension captures the extent to which individual needs and goals are prioritized over group needs and goals.

-Power distance: This dimension measures the degree to which less powerful members of a society accept and expect that power is distributed unequally.

-Masculinity vs. femininity: This dimension describes the degree to which traditional gender roles and expectations are emphasized in a culture.

-Uncertainty avoidance: This dimension captures the extent to which a culture is comfortable with uncertainty and ambiguity.

-Long-term vs. short-term orientation: This dimension describes the extent to which a culture values long-term planning and perseverance over short-term results.

The impact of Hofstede's dimensions of cultural variability is that they provide a framework for understanding and comparing the values, beliefs, and behaviors of different cultures. This can help businesses, governments, and other organizations to better understand the cultural differences they may encounter when working with people from different parts of the world. It can also help individuals to better understand and adapt to the cultures they encounter when traveling or working in other countries.

15. what do you think are the advantages and disadvantages of homogenization and heterogenization of culture and impact to the society. pa help Naman pls​


Answer:

There are others, but the main advantage of a homogeneous culture that I can think of is this: simply, people trust each other instinctively and conveniently because of a simple everyday awareness of each other's humanity.  

When a population has all grown up in the same media, and under nearly the same conditions, and with roughly the same etiquette and knowledge of how the institutions of culture operate, it is simpler for them to empathize with each other. People seem to be kinder to those they understand, and in a homogeneous society, most people understand you fairly well.

When a slight is committed, people appear to be more sympathetic to the perpetrator. There is a common misconception in these cultures that people are mostly "healthy" with just a few "poor apples" to blame for much of society's problems.

Advantages

1. Smoother and potentially improved communication efficiency (though it is possible the distance forces one to evaluate one's own culture more closely, and to ultimately improve how well you express its values).

2. More global collaboration on the basis of mutual vision of the environment, shared goals, shared methodologies for achieving them. Global collaboration is likely to involve a degree of similarity, ideals are expressed in common societies.

3. A wider demand for entertainment (music, media, etc.) that responds to common desires and fads. Achieved economies of scale. Many choices for customers.

4. Greater predictability of the behavior of others.

Disadvantages

1. Less variety of proposals. Different societies are powerful tests to see how a society might function and offer examples to test current values and the status quo. They offer inspiration for changes within a given society.  

2. Less freedom to leave your own society if you don't like it.

3. Flameness of uniformity. Many of them are curious to learn how a radically new method operates.

4. There may be an aspect of "specialization" that arises from possessing diverse cultures. There may then be a net economic loss from this aspect (in isolation)

Heterogenization

It depends on how it's received. The advantages are that people have access to the latest they have from a certain perspective. The drawbacks are that they can spark feuds. In the other side, people will compete with each other (actually it might be more normal when they're only near enough to compete for the same space and not close enough to feel like "family").
