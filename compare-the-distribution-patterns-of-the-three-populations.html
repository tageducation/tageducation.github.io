Size and density are helpful indicators for describing populations. Scientists can learn more about species biology and ecology by observing how individuals are distributed in space. The geographic relationship between members of a population within a habitat can be seen in dispersion or distribution patterns. Patterns are frequently indicative of a specific species; they depend on the local environmental circumstances, the species' growth traits (as in the case of plants), or its behavior (as for animals).

A population's members can be dispersed in one of three fundamental ways: uniformly spaced apart (uniform dispersion), randomly dispersed without a discernible pattern (random dispersion), or clustered in clusters (clumped dispersion).

→ You can see the next Figure in the picture below.

In the next figure, there are three types of distribution patterns among populations of organisms: A population's distribution could be uniform, random, or clumped. Penguins and other territorial birds typically have a consistent distribution. Dandelions and other plants with wind-dispersed seeds typically have a random distribution. Animals that migrate in packs, like elephants, display clumped distribution.

In plant species that restrict the growth of neighboring individuals, uniform dispersion is shown. An illustration of negative allelopathy is the release of poisons by the sage plant, Salvia leucophylla. The toxins kill off the surrounding vegetation in a circle around each sage plant, resulting in an even spacing between them. Uniform dispersion is also present in animals with specified territories, such as nesting penguins.

Dandelion and other plants with wind-dispersed seeds that germinate wherever they fall in a good environment cause random dispersion. Plants that drop their seeds directly to the ground, like oak trees, or animals that gather in packs, like fish schools or elephant herds, exhibit clumped dispersion. Habitat heterogeneity may also lead to clumped dispersions. As with lions congregating around a watering source, organisms have a tendency to clump together in areas where favorable conditions are concentrated.

In this way, the distribution of individuals within a population tells us more about their interactions with one another and their surroundings than does a mere assessment of density. When compared to social species that congregate in groups, solitary species with a random distribution may experience comparable difficulties in locating mates as lower-density species do.
