1. Enumerate and describe the difference parts of a typhoon


Answer:

Two main parts of a Typhoon/Hurricane based on function would be; The Eye and Everything Else.

The massive bulk of the storm is made up of thunderstorm cells, Cumulonimbus, abbreviated CB. Warm air rises. The CB is formed as the heat from the warm water and water vapour from the ocean rise. As it rises it creates an updraft, it cools, water vapour condenses into water droplets that forms the cloud, condensation releases more heat into the cloud, strengthening the updraft. Rain drops form when the water droplets get too big and they fall, dragging the air down with them, creating a downdraft. The up and down drafts create violent winds in and around each CB, it can spawn tornadoes, hail, lightning. Each cell can feed off other cells as well as the heat from the water.

The Eye is formed as a result of all the air that the CBs pushed up to the tropopause, thats where the CB stop rising and the cloud spread out into its anvil shape. All that air has to go somewhere, it converges in the center of the storm and gets pushed down, as air is forced downwards it is compressed, this warms it, the water droplets of the cloud evaporates and the sky in the Eye becomes clear.

This is a rough explanation, there are many other things going on in a Typhoon/Hurricane, but thats the quick version.

20. Enumerate and describe the different parts of a typhoon.​


Typhoon

The rainbands, eye, and eyewall make up a typhoon's core components. In the northern hemisphere (and clockwise in the southern hemisphere), air spirals inward toward the center and then out the top in the opposite direction. Air sinks in the storm's core, creating an almost entirely cloudless "eye."

Further explanation

One's Eye

The core of a typhoon is often a 20–40 mile (32–64 km) wide region of sinking air, normally clear skies, and mild winds that seldom exceed 15 mph (24 km/h). The calmest area of the storm, the eye, often forms when the greatest sustained wind speeds exceed 74 mph (119 km/h).

Its Eyewall

The eyewall is where the powerful wind can reach as near as it can. A ring of towering thunderstorms that form the eyewall deliver intense rainfall and often have the greatest winds. Wind speed, a measure of the storm's strength, can alter due to changes in the eye and eyewall's structure.

Rainbands

cloud bands with curves and thunderstorms that spiral outward from the eye wall. Tornadoes as well as strong wind and rain gusts can be generated by these bands. There are occasionally spaces between spiral rain bands with no rain or wind.

In fact, if one were to travel from a hurricane's outer edge to its center, one would typically progress from light rain and wind to dry and weak breeze before returning to increasingly heavier rain and stronger wind over and over again, with each period of rain and wind being more intense and lasting longer.
