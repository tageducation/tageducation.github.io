There's so many data-gathering techniques and based on my research here's the main or 3 main data-gathering technique:

1. Observation

2. Surveys/Questionnaires

Explanation:

1. Observation

—The observations are recorded in field notes or on a mobile device if the observer is collecting data electronically (like with Fulcrum).

Some examples of observational data collection are building inspections, safety checklists, agricultural surveys, and damage assessments.

Observation is an effective method because it is straightforward and efficient: It doesn’t typically require extensive training on the part of the data collector, and he or she is generally not dependent on other participants.

The biggest drawback of observational data is that it tends to be superficial and lack the context needed to provide a complete picture.

2. Surveys/Questionnaires

— Questionnaires are a popular means of data collection because they are inexpensive and can provide a broad perspective. They can be conducted face-to-face, by mail, telephone, or Internet. Surveys are often used when information is sought from a large number of people or on a wide range of topics. They can contain yes/no, true/false, multiple choice, scaled, or open-ended questions or all of the above.

3. Interviews

—Interviews can be conducted in person or by phone, and can be structured (using survey forms) or unstructured. But there are also many benefits to interviews: They don’t require literacy on the part of the respondents, for one thing. For another, they allow the interviewer (especially a well-trained one) to uncover deep insight by clarifying and deep-diving into the respondent’s answers, as well as by collecting nonverbal data.

What is Data Gathering?

The procedure of collecting, measuring, and evaluating correct insights for research using established approved procedures is referred to as data collection. On the basis of the facts gathered, a researcher might evaluate their hypothesis. Regardless of the subject of study, data collecting is usually the first and most significant phase in the research process. Depending on the information needed, different approaches to data gathering are used in different disciplines of study.

The most important goal of data collecting is to collect information-rich and accurate data for statistical analysis so that data-driven research decisions may be made.

While methods and goals may vary per field, the overall data collection procedure is largely the same. You should think about the following things before you start collecting data:

The study's goal is to:The type of information you'll gatherThe techniques and methods you'll employ to acquire, store, and process data.The methods and procedures you will use to gather, store, and process the data

Techniques for gathering data

There are various tools that can be used to collect data under the three main groups of research methods (quantitative, qualitative, and mixed). Interviews might take place in person or over the phone. Paper or web-based surveys/questionnaires are available. Observations and experiments can be used to collect quantitative, qualitative, or a combination of the two types of information. Other scholars can use records to look for prior knowledge.

What do qualitative methodologies entail?

In-depth interviews, focus groups, ethnographic research, content analysis, and case study research are examples of qualitative research methodologies that are commonly utilized. Qualitative approaches produce more descriptive results, and inferences may be derived from the data with relative ease.

How to Gather Quantitative Information

Quantitative research usually necessitates a high sample size. This is due to the fact that the findings of your study will be representative of a broader population.

Quantitative techniques

You can acquire quantitative data using a variety of approaches, including:

ExperimentsObservations under strict controlPaper, kiosk, mobile, and questionnaire surveys are all available.Longitudinal researchPollsInterviews over the phoneFace-to-face interviews are a great way to learn more about a company

Mixed Method

Mixed methods research is a type of study in which researchers collect and analyze both quantitative and qualitative data.  The growth of mixed methods research in nursing and healthcare has coincided with an increase in the complexity of healthcare delivery on a global scale. Mixed methods research takes advantage of the benefits of both qualitative and quantitative methods, allowing researchers to look at things from many angles and identify connections between the layers of our numerous research issues.

-Quantitative research is used when a researcher want to have more statistical result on their research. They want a precise number on their research. The data gathering that could be used in quantitative research is with questionnaire. 

-Mixed-method research is a combination of both quantitative and qualitative research. The data gathering that could be used in mixed-method are questionnaire, interview, and observation.

Interviews

Interviews can be conducted in person or by phone, they can be structured (using interview guidance) or unstructured. However, there are numerous advantages to conducting interviews: For one thing, they enable the interviewer to gain deep insight by clarifying and delving into the respondent's responses, as well as collecting nonverbal data. For another, they enable to have more detail assessment.

Observation

One of the earliest and most basic ways to qualitative research methodology is observation. This method entails methodical and relevant data collection using one's senses, particularly looking and listening. Because it is simple and effective, observation is a good strategy to use when gathering data. The data collector usually doesn't need much training and is usually independent of other participants. 

Questionnaires/Survey

A questionnaire/survey is suitable for certain research questions. Its applicability is determined by the types of data required to address a research issue and the subjects the researcher wants to interview. Surveys should not be used to gather data on delicate subjects like sexual orientation or illegal behavior.
