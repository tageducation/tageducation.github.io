What is weathering?

The breakdown or dissolving of rocks and minerals on the Earth's surface is referred to as "weathering." Water, ice, acids, salts, plants, animals, and temperature variations are some of the weathering agents. Following the breakdown of a rock, erosion is the process that removes the mineral and rock shards.

By coming into touch with water, gases in the atmosphere, and living things, rocks, soils, minerals, wood, and artificial objects all deteriorate through weathering.

The degradation of rocks close to the surface of the Earth brought on by precipitation, temperature changes, and biological activity is known as weathering. It does not include clearing away rubbish linked to rocks. There are three distinct types of weathering: physical, chemical, and biological.

Rocks and minerals undergo weathering when they are broken down by the wind, water, ice, and heat. In order to produce the many various types of landforms found on the surface of the earth, weathering is a crucial process. For instance, weathering is what creates caves, canyons, and mountains.

The atmosphere, water, and plant and animal life are what cause weathering most often. Rock's surface minerals are dissolved and made more brittle by weathering so that erosive forces like water, wind, and ice can carry them away. Weathering can be either mechanical or chemical.
