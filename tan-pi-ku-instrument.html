8. tan(π/8)-tan3(pi/8)+tan5(π/8)-...=


Answer:

The infinite sum is equal to √2/4.

Step-by-step explanation:

Observe that the terms on the infinite sum form a geometric sequence wherein the initial term is tan(π/8) and the common ratio is -tan²(π/8).

We have the formula for the infinite geometric sequence:

[tex]\displaystyle \sum \limits_{n\geq 1}a_n=\frac{a}{1-r}; \ |r|<1[/tex]

Note that by half-angle formula of tangent,

[tex]\begin{gathered}a=\tan\Big(\frac{\pi}{8}\Big)=\frac{1-\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})}{\sin(\frac{\pi}{4})}=\frac{1-\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}}{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}=\sqrt{2}-1 \end{gathered}[/tex]

It follows that

[tex]\begin{gathered} r=-\tan^2\Big(\frac{\pi}{8}\Big)=-(\sqrt{2}-1)^2=-(3-2\sqrt{2})=2\sqrt{2}-3\end{gathered}[/tex]

Since the absolute value of the common ratio is less than 1, we can use the formula for the infinite geometric sequence.

Recall the first term is √2 - 1 and the common ratio is 2√2 - 3.

[tex]\begin{gathered}\sum_{n\geq1}a_n=\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{1-(2\sqrt{2}-3)}=\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{4-2\sqrt{2}}=\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{2\sqrt{2}(\sqrt{2}-1)}=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}\end{gathered}[/tex]

Therefore, the infinite sum is equal to 1/2√2 or √2/4 when rationalized.
