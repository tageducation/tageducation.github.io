1. why are carbon atoms able to form many organic compounds


Why are carbon atoms able to form many organic compounds  

More than 10 million organic compounds known to chemists are now available. There are several more in  nature, and organic chemists constantly produce new ones. Carbon is the only element that can create so many different compounds since each carbon atom can form four chemical bonds to other atoms and since it is the right carbon atom, small in size, as components of very large molecules.

With the atomic number 6, each carbon atom has a total of six electrons. The remaining four are valence electrons — external electrons that are necessary to interact with other nuclei. Three are in complete orbit within.

The four valence electrons of the carbon atom can be exchanged with other atoms, which exchange electrons, forming covalent (exchanged) relations. They can also be exchanged by other carbon atoms, which, in effect, can share electrons with other carbon atoms, and so on, forming long chains of carbon atoms, linked together like chain links. Iflicon (Si) also contains four valence electrons, an additional factor in Group 14 of the periodical table, which can create large molecules known as sili cones, but the atoms are too large to fit as many molecules as carbon atoms do.

1. The ability of carbon to form long carbon-to-carbon chains is the first of five reasons that there can be so many different carbon compounds; a molecule that differs from one atom is, of course, a molecule of another compound.

2. The second explanation for the extraordinary ability of carbon compound is that carbon atoms can not only bind in straight chains, but in complex branches, such as the branches of a tree. You can also create rings of carbon atoms with "head-to-tail" caps. The number or complexity of the branches and the number of rings attachable to them is practically not limited; hence, the number of different molecules that can be formed is not restricted.

3. The third explanation is, carbon atoms do not only share one electron with another atom, they can also share a double or triple bond with two or three electrons. This implies a great number of possible link combinations at various sites, allowing a large number of possible molecules. And a molecule that varies by one atom or bond is another compound molecule.

4. The fourth reason is that the same collection of atoms and bonds, but in a different geometrical arrangement within the molecule, makes a molecule with a different shape and therefore different properties. These various molecules are called isomers.

5. Fifthly, all electrons which are not used for the attachment of carbon atoms into chains and rings can be used to form links with atoms of different elements. The other element is hydrogen, which makes the hydrocarbon family known. But as part of an organic molecule it is possible to also contain nitrogen, oxygen, phospore, phosphorus, halogens and many other atoms.
