10. 4. It is where meteors can be found.​


Meteors can be found:

1. Willamette

2. Mbozi

3. Cape York

4. Bacubirito

EXPLANATION:

1. Willamette

Willamette is the largest meteorite ever found in the US, measuring 7.8 square meters and weighing 15.5 tons. The Willamette meteorite is made of iron and nickel and was acquired by the American Museum of Natural History in New York City in 1906. It was discovered by Ellis Hughes in 1902 who realized it was more than a lump of rock, and spent three months moving it three quarters of a mile. of land owned by the Oregon Iron and Steel Company, but he was caught.

2. Mbozi

Mbozi was discovered in Tanzania in the 1930s. It is 3 meters long and weighs an estimated 25 tons, or almost twice that of the Willamette. Mbozi was once a sacred stone for the Tanzanian people, who called it kimondo. No crater was found, meaning it probably rolled like a boulder when it hit the Earth's surface. Mbozi was partially buried when it was first discovered, so people dug up the slopes around it, leaving piles of earth beneath, which were later turned into plinths.

3. Cape York

The third largest meteorite in history, the Cape York meteorite, collided with Earth nearly 10,000 years ago. The Cape York meteorite, or Agpalilik meteorite, was discovered in 1993 in Greenland and weighed about 20 tons. It's been around for a long time, and the Inuit who live nearby use other peces as mental resources for tools and spears. It is currently on display at the Geological Museum of the University of Copenhagen, Denmark.

4. Bacubirito

The Bacubirito meteorite is the largest meteorite ever found in Mexico and weighs roughly the same as Cape York. The Bacubirito meteorite was funded in 1863 by geologist Gilbert Ellis Bailey in the village of Ranchito near the town of Sinaloa de Leyva. He had been sent there by the Chicago journal the Interocean to Central and South America, and excavated the meteorite with the help of local residents. It is an iron meteorite that weighs about 20 tons, and measures 4.25 meters long, 2 meters wide, and 1.75 meters high. It is currently on display at the Centro de Ciencias de Sinaloa.
