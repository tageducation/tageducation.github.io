10. How traditions become contemporary and becomes traditional?​


Answer:

Anything traditional can become contemporary by reinterpreting its function, use, pragmatism, or even ethics and morality. Sometimes traditions outwear their longevity because the original reason for their being no longer applies. Example: it used to be traditional that people used outhouses to go to the toilet; not a good tradition if it was sub-zero temperatures in the night! Now people have WCs (water closets) installed in the interior of their homes.

However, folk lore and folk wisdom can be embedded in folk traditions - which can reappear on a cyclic basis, such as the re-acceptance of eating garlic on a daily basis, or the re-emergent herbalism.

I have heard of one family’s tradition that they always had to use a square roasting pan for their Sunday roast. After a few generations of the family had done it, someone thought to ask why? It appears there was no good reason for doing it now - it was initially done because the great grandmother had an oven that couldn’t accept a rectangular pan. The contemporary version of that tradition was to go out and purchase a more accommodating roasting pan.

It was once traditional for absolutely everybody, down to newborn babies, young kids, teens, and adults to wear a head covering. People simply did not exit the home without it. There was even a large supply of extraordinary hatpins to keep some of the wilder hat creations on people’s heads. Now it seems to be optional. Some people do, some people don’t. Probably, in the way of things, there will come a time when it is again fashionable, and people will point to the previous tradition, and say that, of course, everybody should wear a hat.
