3. what is the total momentum of the system before collision?


Momentum is the quantity of motion of a moving object. It can be measured through multiplying its mass by its velocity. There is a law in physics called the "law of momentum conservation", where it states that the total momentum of the two objects before collision is equal to their total momentum after collision. In a collision happening between object 1 and object 2, the momentum lost by object 1 is equal to the momentum obtained by object 2.

This statement will show us that the total momentum of all the given objects is  conserved. It is another way of saying that the total amount of momentum is an unchanging value or constant.

6. what is the total momentum of the system before collision brainly


The total momentum of the system can be solve by adding the momentum of two colliding object. In some point, the total momentum can be conserved. The total momentum of the system is conserved when there is no net force acting on it like friction and gravity. The net force or an unbalance force will change the momentum of the system thereby total momentum is not conserved. Momentum is somewhat related to the newton's first law of motion (law of inertia) which states that a moving object will continue moving unless acted upon by a net force and an object at rest will remain at rest unless acted upon by a net force (unbalance force).

Example problem of computing total momentum before collision

Problem:

Two 0.5 kg balls approach each other with the same speed of 1.0 m/s.

What is the total momentum of the system before collision?

Given Data:

Object 1                                Object 2

m (mass)  = 0.5 kg                m (mass) = 0.5 kg

v (velocity) = 1.0 m/s             v (velocity) = -1.0 m/s (opposite w/ object 1)

p (momentum) = ?                 p (momentum) = ?

Solution:

Object 1                                             Object 2

p = mv                                                  p = mv

p = 0.5 kg × 1.0 m/s                            p = 0.5 kg × -1.0 m/s

p = 0.5 kg.m/s                                     p = -0.5 kg.m/s

Total momentum = p(object 1) + p(object 2)

                             = 0.5 kg.m/s  + (-0.5 kg.m/s)

                             = 0 kg.m/s

Answer:

The total momentum of the system is 0 kg.m/s

11. What is the total momentum before and after collision?


Answer:

The total momentum of the system before the collision is equal to the total  momentum of the system after collision.

Example:

Two ice skaters stand together. They “push off” and travel directly away from each  other, the boy with a velocity of 1.50 m/s. If the boy weighs 735 N and the girl, 490  N, what is the girl’s velocity after they push off? (Consider the ice to be frictionless.)

Solution:

Remember that W = mg, thus, m = W/g

Given:

                               mass                      Velocity

Boy                         75 kg                       1.50 m/s

Girl                       50 kg                            ?

The ice where they stand on is considered to be frictionless, thus, no external force is  present. The momentum of the boy-girl system is conserved. There is no change in the  momentum of the system before and after the push off.

Total Initial Momentum = Total Final Momentum

                                0       = p(boy) + p(girl)  

                          - p(boy)  = pgirl

                         -(mv)boy = (mv)girl

                   -112.5 kg m/s = 50 kg (vgirl)

      -112.5 kg m/s / 50 kg = vgirl

                          - 2.25 m/s = vgir

The girl moves at 2.25 m/s in opposite direction to the boy.

Momentum is a vector quantity. It must have both magnitude (numerical value) and  direction. The direction of the momentum vector is always in the same direction as the velocity  vector. Like all vector quantities, momentum vectors can be added. For situations in which the  two vectors are in opposite directions, one vector is considered negative and the other  positive.

2. Inelastic collision – one in which the total kinetic energy of the system

13. if there is no external force acting on the system, what is the total momentum of the system after collision?


Momentum is a quantity that shows a measure of the difficulty of stopping the motion of an object. Stopping an object takes work equal to the change in its mechanical energy.

The greater the mass of an object, the greater the work required to stop the object. The greater the object's mass, the greater the object's momentum. Likewise with speed. The greater the speed of an object, the greater the work required to stop the object, and the greater the object's momentum.

There are two types of momentum:

- Linear momentum is the momentum of an object that is moving in translation (moving in a straight line).

- Angular momentum is the momentum of a rotating object (moving in a circle or rotating)


Since momentum is directly proportional to mass and velocity, it can be formulated as follows:

[tex]p = m v[/tex]

Where p is defined as momentum (kg m/s), m is defined as mass (kg), and v is defined as speed (m/s).

At the time of the collision, the law of conservation of momentum will apply, which reads

"If no external forces are acting on the system, then the system's momentum will always be constant."

So the law of conservation of momentum can be formulated as:

[tex]p_{1} + p_{2} = p'_{1} + p'_{2}[/tex]

[tex]m_{1} . v_{1} + m_{2} . v_{2} = m_{1} . v'_{1} + m_{2} . v'_{2}[/tex]

Where m is the object’s mass, v' is the object's velocity after the collision, and v is the object's velocity before the collision.

Based on the collision process, we can determine the coefficient of restitution of a collision. The coefficient of restitution (e) is the value of the ratio between the relative velocity of the object after the collision with the relative velocity of the object before the collision.

[tex]e=\frac{v'_{2} - v'_{1} }{v_{1} - v_{2} }[/tex]

Where e is the coefficient of restitution, v' is the object's velocity after the collision, and v is the object's velocity before the collision.

Hence, the total momentum of the system after the collision if the is no external force acting on the system is the same as the total of the system before the collision because it's an effect of the law of conservation of momentum.

Answer:

If there is no external force acting on the system, the momentum is conserved therefore, the total momentum before and after collision are equal. Total momentum of the system after collision does not change.

Example:

Two children on skateboards are initially at rest. They push each other so that the boy moves to the right while the girl moves in the opposite direction away from each other.  Newton’s Third Law tells us that the force that  the girl exerts on the boy and the force that  makes the girl move in the other direction are  of equal magnitude but opposite direction.  The  boy and the girl make up a system – a  collection of objects that affect one another. Only internal force are applied in the system that is why the momentum before and after the push-off are equal.
