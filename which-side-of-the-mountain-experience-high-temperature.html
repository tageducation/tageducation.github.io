1. The condensation of water is causing clouds to form here. Water condenses and the atmosphere is forced upward by the mountain, causing the water vapor to condense and form rain.

2. windward side

Windward is the direction upwind from the point of reference, or alternatively, the direction from which the wind is blowing. This is the warmer, wetter part of an island. It is subject to consistent winds that blow cool, moist air upward. As a result of the drop in ambient pressure, this air continues to cool as it is lifted (scientifically called adiabatic cooling). Since cooler air cannot contain as much water vapor as warm air, the moisture in the air condenses and clouds form. It is pouring outside.

3. Leeward side

is the direction downwind (or downward) from the reference point. When opposed to the windward side of a rock, the leeward side is usually dry. The hand of a ship that faces leeward is known as its lee side. This is the "lower deck" if the vessel is heeling due to wind speed. In certain cases during the Era of Sail, such as the weather gage, the word weather was used as a synonym for windward. Heat is released into the ambient air when condensation occurs. This is known as latent heat of condensation. Most of the moisture in the air has been condensed out by the time it descends the other side of an island (or mountain). If the air begins to sink into sea level, ambient pressure rises, resulting in an increase in temperature. As a result of all of this (technically known as adiabatic warming), the leeward side is normally colder and drier.

4. When air flows down the leeward sides and becomes colder and drier, it increases in the atmosphere.

5. Rain shadow forms a dry area at the back of the mountain.
