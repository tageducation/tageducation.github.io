13. 3. What difficulty did you encounter in determining polynomials? ​


For an expression to be a polynomial term, any variables in the expression must have whole-number powers (or else the "understood" power of 1, as in x1, which is normally written as x). A plain number can also be a polynomial term. In particular, for an expression to be a polynomial term, it must contain no square roots of variables, no fractional or negative powers on the variables, and no variables in the
denominators of any fractions.

Find expressions of the roots without knowing the actual roots,

Find equations whose values are expressions of the roots.

For example, in the quadratic equation x2−bx+c=0 , we know the values of some expressions of the roots r1+r2=b,r1r2=c .

Note that in these expression, it doesn’t matter which root we consider r1 and r2 . Because of this, we say they are symmetric functions of the roots.

Can we have a non-symmetric function of the roots? Sure: consider r1−r2 . This expression will have two different values, depending on which root we call r1 and which root we call r2 .

Let these two values be p,q . Form the resolvent equation whose roots are p,q : this will be (y−p)(y−q)=0 .

Here’s the punchline: If you expand the resolvent equation, you get y2−(b2−4c)=0 . At this point, you should notice two things:

The discriminant, b2−4ac (remember our quadratic had a=1 ,

The equation can be solved using elementary operations, viz.: y=±b2−4c−−−−−−√ .

Now remember y had two solutions, p and q, and there are two values for the square root. From here, we can solve for the individual roots.

So here’s the basic outline:

Find a nonsymmetric function of the roots with k distinct values,

Let these values be the roots of a resolvent equation of degree k.

The problem is that the number of possible values of a nonsymmetric goes up very quickly.

For example, with n = 3 roots, an arbitrary nonsymmetric function of the roots will have 3! = 6 different values, corresponding to a resolvent of degree 6. Obviously, if you’re trying to solve a degree 3 equation, having to solve a degree 6 equation first seems to be going backward.

The good news in this case is that a clever choice of nonsymmetric functions gives you 6 different values which are all related (I won’t try to reproduce the details here, but this question has come up often enough I’m going to put together a detailed video answer). So that 6th degree resolvent is actually reducible to quadratic, which you can solve, etc.

For n = 4, an arbitrary nonsymmetric function will have 4! = 24 possible values. Again, with a clever choice of nonsymmetric functions, you can reduce these 24 to just 3, and find them by solving a cubic.

However, the wrinkle is this: with 3! = 6 values, you had to find a way of relating sets of three roots (in other words, given one root in the set, you have to have a way of producing the other two roots). With 4! = 24 values, you had to find a way of relating eight roots.

With 5! = 120 values, you need to find a way of relating twenty-four roots.
