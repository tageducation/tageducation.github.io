What I have learned is that quantitative research is a way of learning about a specific group of people, known as a sample population. Scientific research, by quantitative research.  We rely on observed or measured data to investigate questions about the participant population.  

Data are generally collected using structured research tools.

Results are based on a larger sample size representative of the population. Research studies can usually be replicated or repeated because they are reliable. Researchers have well-defined research questions that require objective answers. All aspects of the study are carefully designed before the data are collected. Data are provided in numerical and statistical form, often arranged in tables, charts, figures, or other non-textual formats. Projects can be used to generalize concepts more broadly, predict future outcomes, or investigate causality. Researchers collect numerical data using tools such as questionnaires and computer software.

The great power of quantitative research lies in providing descriptive data that allow, for example,  to capture a snapshot of user populations, but face difficulties in interpreting them. Large sample sizes are required, or a  larger sample is expected to give statistically accurate results. In contrast, the cost of contacting these people and creating a questionnaire is higher than qualitative research, so it is also expensive. However, while information is a contextual factor that helps interpret results, much information is difficult to gather with structured research tools. If this is not done seriously and correctly, survey data may be incomplete and inaccurate.

Type of quantitative design

a. Illustrative Design

-- descriptive status, For example, a researcher studying the income of a company's employees and how it relates to their performance. Surveys are conducted to collect data.  If there is sufficient data on employee income, employee performance is evaluated and compared to income.  

b. Correlation design

-- A correlation design identifies relationships between variables. Example: Relationship between student physical activity and academic performance.

c.  Ex post facto design

-- Used to explore possible relationships between past events and current conditions. An example of this is “How do parents' grades in school affect them?”  childhood obesity? ". i.e.

d. Quasi-experimental design

-- Quasi-experimental studies manipulate independent variables without randomly assigning participants to a condition or sequence of conditions. In between: important types are non-equivalent group designs, pretest - posttest, and interruption.

e. Experimental design

— This design uses random assignment of subjects and experimental manipulation, thus providing more conclusive results. For example, a variety of blended learning for reading comprehension for Elem students.

The problem in a study will determine what type is suitable to be selected. It also determines what tools and methods are suitable to be used to solve research problems.
