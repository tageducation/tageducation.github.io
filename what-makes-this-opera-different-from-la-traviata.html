2. what makes this opera different from La Traviata?


Opera is a form of play wherein the actors and actresses (singers) play the leading roles which includes music and singing. Even though it involves singing and music, it is still different from musical theater. Opera incorporates performing arts, such as acting, scenery, costume, and sometimes dance or ballet.  In Opera, singers employ two styles of singing:

recitative - a speech-inflected styleself-contained arias

La Traviata, on the other hand, is an opera in three acts. It was composed by the Italian composer Giuseppe Verdi. La traviata means “the fallen woman” or “the one who goes astray”. This refers to the main character named Violetta Valéry which is a courtesan. La traviata features the so-called "Sempre libera" which is an entire soprano repertoire.

ACT 1

Violetta Valéry, a courtesan, knows that she will die soon. She attended a party where she was introduced to Alfredo Germont. Alfredo has been captivated by Violetta for a long time. In fact, he ask everyday how was Violette doing. The guests ask Alfredo to propose a toast. He celebrates true love, and Violetta responds in praise of free love. She is touched by his candid manner and honesty. Suddenly she feels faint and restless, and the guests went away. Alfredo remains behind and told Violetta that he likes her by the song “Un dì felice”. It was the first time that Violatte experienced such feeling. As a gesture, she gave him a camellia. She said that when the camellia has faded, Alfredo should return. Full of hope, Alfredo think that he will see Violetta again the other day. On the other hand, Violetta is torn by her emotions. She doesn’t want to die, but at the same time she feels that Alfredo made her realize that she wants to be loved.

ACT 2

Violetta sold her property to afford living in the country with Alfredo. He doesn't know anything about that and when discovered the truth, he immediately leaves for Paris to get money. When Alfredo is in Paris, Violetta was visited by his father, Giorgio Germont and told her to separate from his son, as their relationship threatens his daughter’s impending marriage. While conversing with Violette, Giorgio realizes that Violetta is not after his son’s money. Violette's love for his son is true and unselfish. But Giorgio convinced her that his relationship with his son has no future until Violetta agrees to leave Alfredo forever. Only after her death shall he learn the truth about why she returned to her old life. She accepts the invitation to the ball and writes a goodbye letter to her lover. Alfredo returns from Parisand read the letter. At the ball the news has spread about Violetta and Alfredo’s separation. Violetta and her new lover, Baron Douphol, arrived. Alfredo wins when he played at the gaming table. When everybody has left, Alfredo and Violetta talked, and she shows that she love Baron very much. Alfredo asks the guests to be his witnesses and declares that he doesn’t owe Violetta anything. He said everything he wanted to say about her. Alfredo and Baron fought because of the scene happened.

ACT 3

Violetta has only a few more hours to live. Giorgio wrote to Violetta, informing her that Alfredo was not injured in the duel. Giorgio told Affredo  about Violetta’s sacrifice for their relationship. Alfredo wants to rejoin her as soon as possible. Violetta is afraid that he might be too late (“Addio, del passato”). Alfredo and Violetta reunited and she regained her energy as if she is not dying. At the end, Violetta died happy and the things between her and Alfredo was settled.

The clarity of the plot seems matched by a single moral idea - that enduring love can and does survive despite all efforts to corrupt it. If in Puccini's Madama Butterfly, it is Pinkerton's callous abandonment of Butterfly on which the tragedy hinges, in Traviata it is Alfredo's dogged determination to stand by his fallen woman, against all odds, which brings out a sense of redemption. Brilliantly, only in death, are Violetta's bourgeois values relinquished, and she is redeemed, ultimately, not as a fallen woman, but a risen one.
