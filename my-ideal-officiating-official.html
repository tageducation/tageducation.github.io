15. If you are officiating official how will you officiate the game


Answer:

Pregame

Arrive at the game site early. Never rush to a game. Allow ample time to get there well in advance of game time.

Park your car in a well-lit area near the building in which you dress. Try to leave sufficient space to avoid being boxed in.

Hold a thorough pregame conference. Do it even if you’ve worked with your partners before.

Attend to administrative duties early. Allow enough time to fix problems.

Dress in proper, clean uniform. Look sharp and you’re off on the right foot.

Inspect the playing area. Correct potential hazards before the game.

Conduct a brief meeting with team captains or coaches. Tell the captains that they are the leaders of the team and may be called on to fulfill that role. Keep it brief.

Observe players during pregame practice. Opponents occasionally try to intimidate each other before a game. Nip that in the bud.

Examine your equipment. Blow your whistle, etc., to make sure it is in working order.

Relax. If you appear tense and nervous, the players will recognize it.

An officiating official is known as a referee that manages a sports event, especially on implementing the game rules to keep the game in order. The three types of officials are rulebook attorneys, freelancers, and a combination of both. The official uses a whistle and dictates the rules and mechanics of the game. The officiating official fulfills duties, depending on the sport and the nature of the competition. The role of an officiating official is vital in sports events because they define the success or failure of a game.

24. officiating official in badminton? ​


Badminton is a sport of racket played with rackets to reach a shuttlecock across a net. Although it can be played on larger teams, the most common game forms are "solos" (with one player per side) and "double" (with two players per side). Badminton is often played as a casual outdoor activity in a yard or on a beach; formal games are played in a rectangle within the court. Poi nts are scored by striking the shuttlecock with the racket and landing it within half of the opponent's court.

Each side can only strike the shuttlecock once before it passes the net. The play ends as soon as the shuttlecock hits the floor or if an offender is called by the umpire, service judge, or (in their absence) the opponent.

Shuttlecock is a feathered or (in informal matches) plastic projectile that flies differently from the balls used in many other sports. In particular, the feathers create higher drag, causing the shuttlecock to shrink faster. Shuttlecocks also have a very high speed compared to balls in other racket sports. Flying shuttlecock gives the sport a unique nature.
