5. are sars-cov-2 infection and transmission study on humans unethical


The prospect of a widely available severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) vaccine is an increasingly high priority for an effective response to the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic and an area of intense interest and attention for professionals, politicians, and the public alike. The understandable desire for such a vaccine has led to significant discussion and even some planning for the possibility of human challenge studies (HCS) as a tool for accelerating the process for identifying, testing, and developing an effective vaccine

Typically, undertaking HCS in vaccine development requires that the disease for which a challenge would be introduced either has an available rescue therapy to treat those who become infected or the disease is known to be self-limiting. There is no rescue therapy for SARS-CoV-2 infection, and proponents of HCS have claimed that the infection is likely to be self-limiting and mild in young, healthy volunteers based on current understanding of the infection. If accurate, the basic requirements for undertaking an HCS could be met if conducted with that population. Proponents further argue that such HCS are ethically acceptable in the current pandemic. Most critically, they contend that these studies are likely to speed the development of effective vaccines.

But based on our assessment of these arguments, we disagree. We believe it is unethical to move forward with such trials at the current time. Whereas proponents of these studies suggest that such studies will accelerate the time to approved vaccines, the facts fail to support these claims. HCS to address SARS-CoV-2 face unacceptable ethics challenges, and, further, undertaking them would do a disservice to the public by undermining already strained confidence in the vaccine development process.

25. are the tuskegee syphilis study and diethylstilboestrol study on pregnant woman unethical brainly


Yes, the Tuskegee syphilis study and the diethylstilboestrol (DES) study were both highly unethical.

The Tuskegee syphilis study was a clinical study conducted between 1932 and 1972 by the U.S. Public Health Service in Tuskegee, Alabama. The study followed the natural progression of untreated syphilis in a group of 600 poor, rural African American men, 399 of whom had the disease. The men were not told they had syphilis and were not treated for the disease even after penicillin became available as a cure in the 1940s. This study was conducted without the informed consent of the participants, and it resulted in significant harm to the men and their families.

The DES study was a study that looked at the effects of the synthetic hormone DES on pregnant women and their children. The study was conducted between 1947 and 1971 and involved administering the drug to around 1.5 million pregnant women in the United States and Canada. The study was conducted without the informed consent of the women and their children, and it resulted in harm to both the mothers and the children, many of whom developed a rare vaginal cancer.

Both studies were conducted without the knowledge or consent of the participants, and both resulted in significant harm to the individuals involved. These studies have been widely criticized for the ethical violations that occurred and for the failure of the researchers to obtain informed consent from the participants. They have led to significant changes in the way that clinical research is conducted and have helped to establish guidelines and regulations to protect the rights and welfare of human subjects in research.
