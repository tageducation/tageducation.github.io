Three types of convergent boundaries

Convergent boundaries form when tectonic plates clash, which can happen when two oceanic plates collide, two continental plates collide, or an oceanic plate collides with a continental plate.

1. Oceanic - Continental Convergent Boundaries

When the convergence happens between the oceanic plate and continental plates, the oceanic plate will sink beneath the continental plate because it is heavier. When this happens, the subduction zone will again be a result and along with the continental plate a line where land volcanoes will form. The perfect example would be the Cascadia Subduction Zone which is a result of oceanic plates seducing beneath the North American Plate.

2. Continental - Continental Convergent Boundaries

Continental Plates are tectonic plates found on the crust of the earth. There is no subduction in this type of plate because they are both light in weight. The result of this convergence is mountain belts formed because of the folded crust in the line of convergence. The Himalayas is one example of result of this type of convergence, it resulted from the collision between the Indian Plate and the Eurasian Plate.

3. Oceanic - Oceanic Convergent Boundaries

Oceanic plates are tectonic plates that are underneath the ocean. When they collide with each other, the older and heavier oceanic plate sinks under the newer oceanic plate, this process is called subduction. What marks the subduction zone is a seafloor trench or a long, narrow, and deep channel. This area is often hit by earthquakes and creates a line where underwater volcanoes are created. An example would be the convergence Pacific plate and the Mariana plate which resulted in the subduction zone and a trench called the Mariana Trench which is also the deepest part of the ocean.

KEY POINTS

On a microscopic scale, conduction occurs as rapidly moving or vibrating atoms and molecules interact with neighboring particles, transferring some of their kinetic energy.

Conduction is the most significant form of heat transfer within a solid object or between solids in thermal contact.

Conduction is most significant in solids, and less though in liquids and gases, due to the space between molecules.

The rate of heat transfer by conduction is dependent on the temperature difference, the size of the area in contact, the thickness of the material, and the thermal properties of the material(s) in contact.
