17. Reflect on this: Professional billiard players like Efren "Bata" Reyes apply the law of conservation of momentum in playing billiards. Using the Law of Conservation of Momentum, how do you think billiard players strategize their gameplay?​


Answer:

When a moving object hits a non-moving object, a lot of its momentum is transferred to the object that was hit. After the first object hits the second, the second starts to gain momentum while the other starts to lose it. A perfect example of this scenario can be scene in playing billiards. When the cue ball hits a billiard ball, the billiard ball starts moving ang the cue ball stops. This is because the cue ball has transferred its momentum to the billiard ball.

Billiard players strategize their gameplay by thinking about how much force they want to use when striking the cue ball. This will vary depending on how far the billiard ball is and how far the pocket is. When aiming for a ball that is farther, a billiard player would apply less force on the cue ball so that it hits the target just right. The player would then do the opposite for a target that is closer.

23. how will you apply strategic analysis and intuitive thinking in the uses technology that you will not become a slave on it?​


Strategic analysis involves evaluating the potential risks and benefits of using a particular technology, and considering how it fits into the overall goals and objectives of an organization or individual. Intuitive thinking, or "gut feeling," can also be a valuable tool in making decisions about technology use. Here are some steps that can help you apply strategic analysis and intuitive thinking to avoid becoming a slave to technology:

1. Identify your goals: What do you want to achieve through the use of technology? Are you using it to increase productivity, streamline processes, or improve communication? Having clear goals will help you evaluate the suitability of different technologies and make informed decisions about their use.

2. Assess the costs and benefits: Consider the costs of using a particular technology, including financial, time, and resource costs, as well as any potential risks or downsides. Compare these costs to the potential benefits of using the technology to determine if it is worth pursuing.

3. Evaluate the fit: Consider how well the technology aligns with your goals and the needs of your organization or individual. Will it help you achieve your objectives, or will it create additional problems or challenges?

4. Trust your intuition: While data and analysis are important, sometimes your gut feeling can also provide valuable insights. If something doesn't feel right about a technology, it's worth taking the time to explore why and considering alternative options.

By applying strategic analysis and intuitive thinking, you can make informed decisions about technology use that support your goals and help you avoid becoming a slave to it.

26. How do strategic analysis and intuitive thinking affect our everyday life?​


Answer:

There are at least two modes styles of thinking that, in my experience, managers appear to follow in developing their strategy, whether business or technology strategy. Some mangers prefer an intuitive style of strategic thinking, while others favour a more analytical style.

Before getting into what I mean by intuitive or analytical (although this is likely to be already obvious to you) it is important to point out that there is no clear winner between the two. Individually, each one has contexts and situations that it is particularly suitable for. It is also important to note that people have different personality ‘make-ups’ (as Myers-Briggs[1] will suggest), and some people are naturally analytical while others are intuitive. As such, to elevate one mode of thinking over the other is, perhaps, to declare a significant fraction of human society better than another – which I am never keen on.

Nevertheless, there are strengths and weaknesses associated with both styles of thinking and it is important to identify these. By knowing and combining these strengths, especially where the combination helps overcome weaknesses of individual styles, managers may begin to find for themselves, more effective ‘sweet spots’ between analytical and intuitive thinking for addressing strategic issues.
