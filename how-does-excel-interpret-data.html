We used Excel to do some basic data analysis tasks to see whether it is a reasonable alternative to using a statistical package for the same tasks.  We concluded that Excel is a poor choice for statistical analysis beyond textbook examples, the simplest descriptive statistics, or for more than a very few columns.  The problems we encountered that led to this conclusion are in four general areas:

Missing values are handled inconsistently, and sometimes incorrectly.

Data organization differs according to analysis, forcing you to reorganize your data in many ways if you want to do many different analyses.

Many analyses can only be done on one column at a time, making it inconvenient to do the same analysis on many columns.

Output is poorly organized, sometimes inadequately labeled, and there is no record of how an analysis was accomplished.

Excel is convenient for data entry, and for quickly manipulating rows and columns prior to statistical analysis. However when you are ready to do the statistical analysis, we recommend the use of a statistical package such as SAS, SPSS, Stata, Systat or Minitab.

5. 3. How does Excel interpret data?​


Excel will interpret the type of data you enter in the worksheet and apply the proper format to that data.

 

These are the 4 functions of Microsoft Excel for data processing.

1. To create good graphics

In fact, sometimes it's easier to understand a topic when you can visualize it properly. Microsoft Excel enables businesses to see and explore the potential of their data by using formulas across a grid of cells. Data is entered into individual cells in rows or columns that allow it to be sorted and filtered, which can then be presented as a visual presentation. The data is visualized in the form of charts available in excel, such as column charts, line charts, pie charts, area charts, scatter charts, stock charts, surface charts, donut charts, bubble charts, and radar charts.

2. To use with Conditional Formatting

Needless to say, working in front of a screen full of numbers all day can cause you to feel as though the numbers are mingling together. Fortunately, Microsoft Excel allows you to be able to use different colors and various types of fonts in your data. You can select the appropriate coloring scheme via the quick analysis button and then take advantage of the formatting tab. These tools really make it easy for you to understand the data because being able to highlight the features of the data will make your work easier.

3. To Assist in Data Identification

When you present or visualize data in the form of graphs, it really helps you to see the details of trends explicitly emerging from the information. That way, you can be helped to see the important points of the data that you have processed in a relatively easy way. Microsoft Excel allows trend lines to be extended beyond charts to offer predictions of future activity, and these forecasts can help you develop your business with specific strategies for the company's future.

4. Simple Data Entry and Formatting

Data collection offers businesses great value. With the right data, businesses can determine what strategies are working, how they work and what the team needs to do to improve these results. When predicting data for business purposes, you usually don't think of just one or two samples but hundreds or even thousands of data points. This is where Excel can help you.

6. How does MS Excel evaluate a formula?​


Excel has a handy feature called Evaluate Formula, which solves a formula one step at a time. Each time you click the Evaluate button, Excel will solve the underlined part of the formula and show you the result. You can find Evaluate Formula on the Formulas tab of the ribbon, in the Formula Auditing group.
