11. 5 Examples of Joint Variation in Real life..1.2.3.4.5.5 Examples of Combined Variation in Real life..1.2.3.4.5.​


Joint variation refers to a type of variation in which two or more variables are related such that they vary directly or inversely with each other. Here are five examples of joint variation in real life:

The amount of time it takes to drive a certain distance is inversely proportional to the speed at which one is driving. For example, if you are driving at a speed of 60 mph, it will take you less time to travel a certain distance than if you were driving at a speed of 30 mph.The volume of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature (at a constant pressure). This means that if you heat a gas, its volume will increase.The weight of an object is directly proportional to its mass. This means that if you increase the mass of an object, its weight will also increase.The resistance of a wire is inversely proportional to its cross-sectional area. This means that a wire with a larger cross-sectional area will have a lower resistance than a wire with a smaller cross-sectional area.The speed of sound in a medium is directly proportional to the square root of the medium's temperature. This means that if you increase the temperature of a medium (such as air), the speed of sound will increase.

Combined variation refers to a type of variation in which one variable is related to two or more other variables in a more complex way than just a simple proportionality. Here are five examples of combined variation in real life:

The amount of electricity that can be generated by a hydroelectric dam is a function of the volume of water flowing through the dam and the height of the dam.The resistance of a resistor is a function of its length, cross-sectional area, and the type of material it is made of.The strength of a concrete beam is a function of the size and shape of the beam, as well as the type of concrete used.The efficiency of a car engine is a function of the design of the engine, the type of fuel it uses, and the conditions under which it is operating.The brightness of a light bulb is a function of the type of bulb, the wattage of the bulb, and the distance from the bulb to the observer.

A GENERAL NOTE: JOINT VARIATION

Joint variation occurs when a variable varies directly or inversely with multiple variables.

For instance, if x varies directly with both y and z, we have x = kyz. If x varies directly with y and inversely with z, we have [latex]x=\frac{ky}{z}[/latex]. Notice that we only use one constant in a joint variation equation.

EXAMPLE 4: SOLVING PROBLEMS INVOLVING JOINT VARIATION

A quantity x varies directly with the square of y and inversely with the cube root of z. If x = 6 when y = 2 and z = 8, find x when y = 1 and z = 27.

SOLUTION

Begin by writing an equation to show the relationship between the variables.

[latex]x=\frac{k{y}^{2}}{\sqrt[3]{z}}[/latex]

Substitute x = 6, y = 2, and z = 8 to find the value of the constant k.

[latex]\begin{cases}6=\frac{k{2}^{2}}{\sqrt[3]{8}}\hfill \\ 6=\frac{4k}{2}\hfill \\ 3=k\hfill \end{cases}[/latex]

Now we can substitute the value of the constant into the equation for the relationship.
