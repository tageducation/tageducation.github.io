Identify human activities that speed up or trigger landslides. 4. Suggest ways to help lessen the occurrence of landslides in the community.​


1. Hazard maps can be used to identify areas prone to hazards brought about by landslides. These maps typically show the likelihood of a landslide occurring in a given area, based on factors such as the slope of the land, the type of soil, and the history of landslides in the region.

2. There are several practical ways of coping with geological hazards caused by landslides:

Evacuating to a safe location if a landslide is imminent or has occurredStaying informed about the risk of landslides in your area and being prepared to take action if necessaryMaking sure that your home or business is located in a stable area, away from steep slopes and areas prone to landslidesCreating a disaster preparedness plan and practicing it regularly

3. Human activities that can speed up or trigger landslides include:

Clearcutting or overgrazing of forests, which can remove vegetation that helps hold soil in placeBuilding on steep slopes or in areas prone to landslides, which can increase the weight on the slope and make it more prone to landslidesOveruse of water, such as excessive irrigation, which can cause soil to become saturated and prone to landslidesExcavation or drilling, which can weaken the stability of slopes

4. Here are a few ways to help lessen the occurrence of landslides in the community:

Planting trees and other vegetation on slopes to help hold soil in placeAvoiding building on steep slopes or in areas prone to landslidesImplementing conservation practices, such as reducing water usage and protecting forests, to help reduce the risk of landslidesImplementing land use policies that limit development in areas prone to landslidesWorking with community organizations and local governments to promote landslide awareness and preparedness.

Question:

In your own idea write a reflection about landslide?

Answer:

A landslide is defined as the movement of a mass of rock, debris, or earth down a slope. Landslides are a type of "mass wasting," which denotes any down-slope movement of soil and rock under the direct influence of gravity. The term "landslide" encompasses five modes of slope movement: falls, topples, slides, spreads, and flows. These are further subdivided by the type of geologic material (bedrock, debris, or earth). Debris flows (commonly referred to as mudflows or mudslides) and rock falls are examples of common landslide types.Almost every landslide has multiple causes. Slope movement occurs when forces acting down-slope (mainly due to gravity) exceed the strength of the earth materials that compose the slope. Causes include factors that increase the effects of down-slope forces and factors that contribute to low or reduced strength. Landslides can be initiated in slopes already on the verge of movement by rainfall, snowmelt, changes in water level, stream erosion, changes in ground water, earthquakes, volcanic activity, disturbance by human activities, or any combination of these factors. Earthquake shaking and other factors can also induce landslides underwater. These landslides are called submarine landslides. Submarine landslides sometimes cause tsunamis that damage coastal areas.
