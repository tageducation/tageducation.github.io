The most intense type of electromagnetic radiation, known as gamma rays, are generated during nuclear fusion or fission reactions, nuclear decay, and the emission of radioactive materials. Gamma radiation, also known as gamma rays, is a stream of extremely high-energy electromagnetic radiation released by an atomic nucleus going through radioactive decay. Gamma rays are inherently bio-hazardous, however they can be regulated and employed for a number of crucial applications, including:

With no need for surgery, it is used to treat cancer. Gamma radiation is used on the cancerous tumor, killing the DNA in the process.Sterilizing surgical equipment is accomplished using it.Furthermore, it is applied to the food industry to eliminate dangerous bacteria.The characteristics of some semi-precious stones can be altered by gamma radiation. White topaz is frequently transformed into blue topaz using this method.

High frequency (or shortest wavelength) electromagnetic radiation with a large amount of energy is known as gamma rays. They can only be stopped by something solid, such a big concrete block or a lead block. Gamma rays have frequencies above 10 Hz and wavelengths below 100 pm. They represent the most powerful type of electromagnetic radiation above 100 keV. Paul Villard, a French chemist studying radium, first noticed gamma rays in the 1900s, but Ernest Rutherford gave them the name "Gamma Rays."
