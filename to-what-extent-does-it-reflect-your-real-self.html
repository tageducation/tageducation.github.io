9. to what extent does it reflect yourself example


Answer:

That’s impossible to answer unless one can define what their “real self” is? Is it what you aspire to be? Is it what you manifest as? Is it what others think you are? C.G. Jung painted an interesting picture of this, identifying us as an amalgam of “Self” and “Ego”. Self is arguably “the real you”. But almost nobody ever bothers to get to know this — because there are some very dark aspects to the Self that many will not explore.

We tend to be just Ego-facades — the “display model” or “performance” that we hope everyone (ourselves included), buys.

Jung spoke of the increasingly individuated person as someone who regularly and voluntarily “confronts their shadow”. This is the mythical archetype of the fire-breathing dragon that rises from time to time to vex the village and eat the livestock. The Hero archetype is the brave individual to forthrightly rides forth to confront the dragon, with those who are “unworthy” being roasted and consumed. But the hero with the right stuff can not only defeat the dragon, but bring back the treasure that dragons for some reason, hoard.

Nobody can even start to know themselves unless they know and confront the dragon within. Anyone can read history walking in the shoes of the hero. But can you read history from the standpoint of the ante-hero or the villain? Because they are no more or less human than you. Hitler was somebody’s little boy, their brother, their comrade-in-arms, their lover, their hero.

Few people go here because “knowing thyself” is truly dodgy business that few are up to. For myself, knowing “who I truly am” is an amalgam of who I aspire to be in my most amazing dream; the bag of snakes I know I can be on my worst possible day; and who I become known as by others.

And the closer the marriage between all of these 3 images of me, the more individuated I am.

Self-reflection is part of the process of self-introspection which is done by looking back and reflecting on various things that have happened in life, such as experiences, habits, and decisions. Self-reflection can help you to live a better life in the future.

Doing self-reflection can open up your opportunity to see the important things from every situation you've been through. When done regularly and in the right way, self-reflection can make your life healthier, more balanced, and happier.

Self-reflection is the process of contemplating and analyzing oneself all the habits, thoughts, feelings, and decisions that have been made during daily life. With it, you learn to understand yourself better and know all your strengths and weaknesses.

However, being aware of your limits will help you take control of the options you choose. You can reduce unnecessary stress and choose to focus on what can be done. This awareness will encourage you to live a more balanced life and have a positive effect on your well-being.

1. Understand your strengths and weaknesses

Self-reflection can help you understand your strengths and weaknesses better. You can then use this to develop your strengths into something concrete skills. Meanwhile, by knowing your shortcomings, you can know your limits in doing something.

2. Finding valuable lessons

Self-reflection can also help you gain new perspectives and lessons from what you've been through. This allows you to be optimistic about the future because you already know that the worst things in the present can be beneficial for the future.

3. Respond to things more wisely

When faced with a situation or problem, we often respond to the situation without thinking. In fact, this can lead to unkind words or reactions that we may regret later. Self-reflection can make you more aware of the consequences of your words and actions. That way, going forward you can consider the best course of action you can take in certain situations.

27. How realistic is your self image? To what extent does it reflect your real self?


Self-image is how you perceive yourself. It is an important element in the idea of self-concept, which is the overarching term for anything that has to do with the self. If you have a realistic image of yourself, then it means how you perceive yourself, internally and externally, is closely attached to what you are in real life. This is of course differentiated from how others see you.

On the other hand, if you have a bloated self-image, it means you are looking at yourself more than what your capabilities are. If you see yourself less than what you really are, then you have a reduced sense of self image.

Elements of Self Imagehow you see yourselfyour interpretation of how others see youhow you would like to be

The bigger the gap is between your perception of the self versus your real self and your ideal self, the more you have to work on. But of course, you must first accept who you are and who you are not, before you can work at improving yourself.
