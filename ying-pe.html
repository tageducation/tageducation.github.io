17. What country is YING PE from?​


Ying Pe / Yi Peng come from Thailand. The traditional lantern release festival known as Yi Peng is just how you've seen it in movies. Particularly Yi Peng's vision is transcendent, and for many people, it holds a special meaning that is unsurpassed by any other holiday during the year. The northern region of Thailand, notably Chiang Mai, is where the Thailand Lantern Festival, also known as Yi Peng, is largely observed. After music, other festive entertainment, or prayer, each participant lets go of their sky lantern at roughly the same moment in the evening. Thousands of lanterns rise at once, rising at different rates and illuminating the night sky to varying degrees. Numerous locations where the releases take place, such as the one at Maejo University, are extremely packed.

Buddhism is a major component of the Yi Peng ceremony. In reality, a lot of people think it has Indian roots and was influenced by the story of the candle-carrying bird that supposedly visited Buddha to discuss merit. Some people now think that paying homage to the Buddha will help them achieve great happiness, purity, and popularity when they reincarnate in the hereafter.

Khom loi or khom fai are the names for the lovely paper lanterns that are launched into the air each year in November. A candle or fire starter is affixed to the thin, rice paper-based material used to make khom loi. The heated air produced by the fire and trapped inside the lantern is what gives the diminutive sky lantern its lift. The fire generates energy as the lantern rises, enabling it to fly into the night sky and float in various directions. You may get khom loi practically anyplace before the celebration starts if you manage to travel to Thailand for the spectacular Yi Ping event. Both neighborhood shops and several street sellers will sell them. Though they might run out, make sure to buy reasonably early on. This celebration attracts a lot of people, so planning ahead and making purchases will be beneficial.
