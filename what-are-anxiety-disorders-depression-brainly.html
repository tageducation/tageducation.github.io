Answer

Anxiety Disorders/Depression

Anxiety disorders or depression are a group of mental illness that are not easily recognizable. These are mental illness that can seriously harm a person if not treated properly. The everyday life of a person having an anxiety disorders or depression would be probably stressing and lifeless. Mental health professionals or doctor's treatment will be very helpful so that the person should take further action to fight depression. Support of the family is also necessary for the person to improve and cope up with normal life.

Main Types of Anxiety Disorders/Depression  Generalized anxiety disorder

Is a mental illness that characterized by too much anxiety, stress and worry about everyday life with no specific or obvious reason to be stressed or worried. A person with symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder has the tendency not to stop worrying for every matter such as family, health, financial matter, work or school. The worry of the person is often has no valid reason or unrealistic.

Specific phobias

A phobia is an anxiety symptom that refers to constant and unrealistic intense fear over a person, object or situation. Specific phobia refers to the unrealistic intense fear of a person to a specific person, object or situation once the person faced with them.  

Examples of Specific Phobia

Animal Phobias [Fear of dogs, snakes, cockroaches and mouse]Situational Phobias [ Going over bridges and tunnels , boarding in an airplane , driving , flying, fear of crowded space and fear of closed or confined space] Natural Environment Phobia  [ Fear of thunder and fear of heights] Other Phobias  [ Fear of costumed clown  and fear of falling down]

Social anxiety disorder

It is also called social phobia. This type of phobia is the most common anxiety disorder to most individuals. A person having a social phobia cannot handle normal situation such as talking to strangers, making eye contact, going to parties and starting conversations. The person feels uncomfortable with the environment and feels such situations as too much to handle. A person might also self-conscious and feel too much worry what the others might judge or say about her/him. Worrying or fearing too much because of a possible negative judgement from a crowd is an example of social phobia.

Panic disorder

It is a disorder for over reacting kind of terror that might cause a person to sweat, uneasy, having chest pain or feel palpitations. Having this disorder might cause the person to have heart attack problems or not being able to breath properly.

Treatment of Anxiety disorders or depression

Treatment prescribed by experts or professionals to someone experiencing an anxiety disorder or depression could be through:

MedicationTherapyCombination of medication and therapy

29. 2. Who gets anxiety disorders/depression and how common are they?


How common are anxiety disorders?
Anxiety disorders are the most common of all mental health problems. Research into these disorders has shown
that up to 1 in 4 adults will have an anxiety disorder in their lifetime, and that up to 1 in 10 people will have an
anxiety disorder each year. These disorders are the number one mental health problem in women, and the
second-most common mental health problem in men, just behind addiction.
Unfortunately, people may suffer for years before seeking help, sometimes avoiding the stigma attached to these
types of mental health issues, and sometimes believing the common misconception that they are simply a sign of
weakness or instability. The fact is, they are medical disorders that can be diagnosed and treated.
Who gets an anxiety disorder?
As mentioned above, anxiety disorders commonly affect both men and women, though they occur more often in
women. They also develop in children.
Though the causes of anxiety disorders are not totally understood, it is believed that they develop due to a mix of
biological factors along with your individual situation, much like other health problems. Some of the risk factors for
developing an anxiety disorder include:
• A traumatic experience as a child. If you have suffered abuse or trauma, or witnessed a traumatic event as a
child, you have a higher risk of developing an anxiety disorder at some point in your life.
• Stress due to a physical illness. Living with a chronic or serious illness, such as cancer, can lead to worries
and anxiety about the future.
• General stress build-up. This can occur as a single big event, or as a series of smaller stressful situations that
trigger anxiety, such as the death of a loved one or ongoing financial worries.
• Your personality. It appears that certain personality types are more likely to develop anxiety disorders than
others. Also, some personality disorders, including borderline personality, can be associated with anxiety
disorders.
• Being related to someone with an anxiety disorder.
• Substance abuse. Drugs or alcohol can cause or worsen anxiety.
