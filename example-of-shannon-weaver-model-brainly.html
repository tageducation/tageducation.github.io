3. example of shannon weaver model​


Shannon Weaver Model

In 1948, Warren Weaver and Claude Shannon developed the Shannon-Weaver model. It is a mathematical model that explains the transmission of information from a transmitter to a receiver.

A telephone call is an example of the Shannon-Weaver model. Here, the caller acts as both the sender and the encoder, converting the message into a format that can be transmitted via wires. The cables serve as the channel since they send data packets to the recipient's phone, which serves as a decoder. When the telephone breaks down, noise may occur while the data travels via the cables. The recipient is the person on the other end who decodes the message and can respond to the caller.

Further explanation

The Shannon weaver model consists of fundamental components like a  a receiver, a decoder, an encoder, source, a medium, and noise. It explains how information travels from a sender to a receiver. A transaction, on the other hand, is a model that represents the sender and the recipient interacting at the same time. They each send and receive messages.

Initially, the Shannon-Weaver model was intended to be a linear model in which data moved from a sender to a receiver. Norbert Wiener changed the linear model into a cyclical one by including feedback.

The concept, which is also known as information theory in the field of engineering, is mostly used to compute transmissions that take place in machines. According to the paradigm, the sender is the one who starts the conversation and the information source. Then, a signal is transmitted from the brain to the lips through the mouth. This signal is eventually received by the recipient after being muddled with other noises and distractions.

The Shannon-Weaver model has a number of advantages. It takes into consideration factors that obstruct clear communication in the first place. As a result, it enables the detection and correction of noise or communication problems. The approach also divides the communication process into manageable chunks, which helps the recipient grasp it better. In addition, it makes communication a two-way process, making it relevant to practically all forms of communication. Lastly, it assesses communication as a measurable process.

27. example situation of shannon weaver model schramm model and transactional model​


Example of Shannon Weaver Model

→ A businessman texts his employee on the phone to inform them of a meeting regarding the marketing of their brand. Due of noise, the employee does not hear the entire message. It goes as follows:

Businessman: Due to a phone network outage or background noise, our meeting is scheduled for 8 a.m.Employee (feedback): When?

Here,

The businessman is the sender.

Encoder: Telecommunications network company

The mobile network is the channel.

As a result of the noise, some text was missed.

Decoder: cellular

Receiver: Employee

In this instance, the noise is the transmission mistake. The businessman receives feedback informing him that the message was delivered in part. Only following the recipient's feedback does he have an opportunity to receive the entire message.

Schramm's Interactive Model Examples

Since input may be given even in newspapers and books, the internet might be considered the best medium for interactive communication. Interactive communication opportunities have risen thanks to the internet, which is continually developing.

Since the model is circular and the senders alternate each time, human-computer contact is now also regarded as interactive communication. → Other examples of interactive communication models include social media, interactive marketing, user-generated content, ATMs, online commerce, etc.

Transactional Model Example

The most versatile type of communication is transactional. Conversations and interactions in daily life also use the transactional model of communication. For communicators with comparable personal and environmental characteristics, it is more effective. For instance, since they are part of the same social structure, communication between people who know each other is more effective.

In a transactional architecture, the media utilized also affects how effectively and reliably a message is transmitted.→ For instance, a person might interpret a message differently depending on whether it is delivered face-to-face or over the phone. It is due to potential message loss during a phone call or a lack of gestures.
