Reflection from a Concave Mirror

When the object is far from the mirror, the image is inverted and at the focal point. The image is real light rays actually focus at the image location). As the object moves towards the mirror the image location moves further away from the mirror and the image size grows (but the image is still inverted). 14. what is the difference between a plane and spherical mirror on the kind of image formed​


Answer:

A plane mirror and a spherical mirror are both types of reflective surfaces, but they produce different types of images because of the way they are curved.

A plane mirror is a flat mirror that has a smooth and even surface. When an object is placed in front of a plane mirror, the image formed is a virtual image that is the same size as the object, but reversed left to right. The image is also located behind the mirror and the distance between the object and the image is equal to the distance between the object and the mirror.

A spherical mirror, on the other hand, is curved. Spherical mirrors can be divided into two types: concave and convex.

A concave mirror is a spherical mirror that is curved inward. When an object is placed in front of a concave mirror, the image formed can be either real or virtual, depending on the distance between the object and the mirror. If the object is placed at a distance greater than the focal point, the image will be virtual, smaller than the object and located behind the mirror. If the object is placed at a distance less than the focal point, the image will be real, inverted and the same size as the object, located in front of the mirror.

A convex mirror, on the other hand, is a spherical mirror that is curved outward. When an object is placed in front of a convex mirror, the image formed is always virtual, smaller than the object and located behind the mirror. Convex mirrors are often used in automobiles, stores, and other places for security reasons because they reflect a wider view of the area behind them.

In summary, a plane mirror forms a virtual image that is the same size as the object, reversed left to right and located behind the mirror. A spherical mirror, on the other hand, forms either a real or a virtual image depending on the distance between the object and the mirror and its curvature, concave or convex.
