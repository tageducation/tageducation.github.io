Further explanation:

Joos (1976) says that there are five types of speech styles. There's the "frozen" style, the "formal" style, the "consultative" style, the "casual" style, and the "intimate". It means that when people want to talk to each other, they can choose from five different styles.

Frozen style, also called fixed speech, is the highest level of communication style. It is often used in respectful situations or formal events like Shakespearean plays, weddings, funerals, and more. It uses complicated sentence structures and words that only experts in that field would know.

The formal style uses formal words and phrases and is more common in writing than in speech. It also doesn't let you use ellipses, contractions, or modal adverbs that qualify.

The consultative style is basically an unplanned speech since the speaker uses the participation and feedback of the audience. The speaker will give some background information, but the listener is always involved.

Casual style, also called informal style, is usually used between friends or even people who know each other well and have things to share. In this type, both the speaker and the listener are free to take part.

When two people who are very close to each other talk, they use an "intimate style." It has a small number of words and a high chance of communicating without words. Just like in casual, both the speaker and the listener are free and easy to join in.
