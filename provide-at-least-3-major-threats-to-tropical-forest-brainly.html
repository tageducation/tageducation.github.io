14. 1. Provide at least 3 major threats to tropical forest?


HUMAN THREATS

The Tropical Rain Forests face the biggest challenge from competing agendas within and lack of knowledge within regulatory bodies. About half of the rainforests in the Earth, from 14% of the Earth to 6%, is already destroyed. Some predict the rainforests will disappear within the next hundred years at the present rate of destruction.

Logging Interests - Trees are cut down to be used in flooring, furniture, and other such items.

Power Plants & Other Industries - Trees are cut down to be burnt to generate electricity.

The Paper Industry - Trees are cut down to be made into the pulp that dries to make paper.

The Cattle Industry - Trees are cut down to make more pastures for cattle to graze in.

Agricultural Interests (EX: The Soy Industry) - Trees are cut down to provide farmland.

Subsistence Farmers - Trees are cut down to provide firewood and make more room to grow crops and raise livestock.

Mining Operations - Trees are cut down to build roads out to new mine sites.

Governments & Industry - Trees are cut down to build service and transit roads.

Hydroelectric Projects - Areas of forest are flooded to aid in the creation of electricity.

NATURAL THREATS

There are also several instances in which nature harms tropical rainforests. I believe that, while people play a large part in the loss of rainforests, it is important to remember that we are not the single reason for the crisis.

Fire - Natural fires in rainforests are typically smaller than average, burning only the ground vegetation and a few of the smaller trees, while the upper layers are spared. Larger fires do occur occasionally, and are generally caused by lightning strikes or human activity during times of drought. An example of this the Bornean Fires of 1982-1983, where 9 million acres of forest in Indonesia were destroyed. Estimates say that over half of the Amazon Rainforest is at risk of burning during extreme droughts.

Volcanic Activity - Volcanic Eruptions can cause fires, burning large tracts of forest or covering them in magma. Eruptions that release large amounts of carbon dioxide can cause many animals to die through asphyxiation, when carbon dioxide settles into dips in the ground. This makes the plants grow well, but is lethal to any animal that happens to wander in. This is a rare occurrence, however, and is generally not considered a major threat to species in the rainforests.

Drought - Droughts initially weaken the canopy of the rainforest by reducing local humidity and rainfall. This will eventually dry out leaf litter, which kills the decomposers and reduces the amount of nutrients plants get, causing many to suffer. Drought can weaken rainforests so they are more susceptible to other events like fire and disease in addition to their own consequences. Many scientists are concerned that global warming could worsen the droughts rainforests experience, hence putting them at an even greater risk of extinction.

Tropical Storms - While smaller tropical storms do not pose much of a problem to rainforests, larger storms (like hurricanes, cyclones, and typhoons) can cause extensive damage. High wind speeds combined with the tightly packed trees can cause large swaths of land to be cleared, which can take centuries to be naturally restored to its former state.
