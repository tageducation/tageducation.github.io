In an ecosystem, food webs are made up of many food chains that are linked to each other and overlap. Food webs show how many different food chains and trophic levels are linked to each other.

1. Hermit crab eats → Mussels and clams, bits of dead animals, and macroalgae.

2. Limpet may be eaten by → Starfish and shore birds like seagulls.

3. Algae may be eaten by → Zooplankton, small fish, and crustaceans.

4. Sea urchin eats → About almost anything that floats by near them.

5. Chitons and limpets may be eaten by → Humans, seashore birds like seagulls, and other sea creatures like starfish and crabs.

The four most important types of organisms in aquatic food chains and webs are: Producers: Bacteria, phytoplankton, and algae are all examples of primary producers. They are at the lowest level of the aquatic food chain and food web, which means they are at the bottom. Phytoplankton use chlorophyll to get energy from the sun. They use photosynthesis to turn the light energy from the sun into the chemical energy they need to live and grow.

Consumers: The next level of the aquatic food chain and food web is made up of herbivores, which eat plants. Many zooplanktons eat phytoplankton, such as copepods, rotifers, and the larval stages of some fish and invertebrates. As they move through the water, they eat phytoplankton. Algae is eaten by marine snails, fish, reptiles, and mammals, among other things.

Large predators are at the top of the food web and aquatic food chain. There are many different kinds of predators, and they eat many different kinds of prey. Animals that eat a lot of other animals are top predators. Killer whales, leopard seals, big sharks, sunflower sea stars, and many kinds of fish like herring, cod, and tuna are some examples.

Scavengers: Parts of animals and organisms that haven't been eaten all the way sink to the bottom. Bottom-dwelling scavengers like crabs and lobsters may eat them. Decomposers are organisms that break down dead plants, animals, and trash to release energy and nutrients back into the ecosystem. This is true of crabs, fungus, worms, and bacteria, among other things.

2.Limpets eat by grazing on algae found on rock surfaces. They scrape films of algae from the rock with a radula, a ribbon-like tongue with rows of teeth.

3.Algae is eaten by everything from the tiniest shrimp to the largest living animal, the blue whale. Fuels used in cars and aeroplanes may be powered by algae in the future. Algae can also be grown as feed for animals and as fertiliser to spread on soil and help crops grow.

4.Sea urchins will eat just about anything that floats by. Its sharp teeth can scrape algae off rocks, and grind up plankton, kelp, periwinkles, and sometimes even barnacles and mussels. Sea urchins are sought out as food by birds, sea stars, cod, lobsters, and foxes.
