14. Why does formalin and acetone belong to the group of carbonyl containing compounds


There are three types of carbonyl-containing group compounds namely aldehyde, ketone, and carboxylic acid.

An aldehyde is an organic compound with a general chemical structure of carbonyl center (C=O) where the carbon is bonded with R group (alkyl or side chain) and H (for hydrogen). All aldehydes end with –al as a recognition of the presence of carbonyl group and the attached hydrogen. Ketone is an organic compound with a general chemical structure of carbonyl center (C=O) and the carbon is bonded with 2 R groups (alkyl or side chain). All ketones end with –one as a recognition of the presence of carbonyl group where the carbon is not at the end of the branch but always within the chain.

Formalin and acetone belong to the carbonyl-containing group compounds because of the presence of C=O in their chemical structure. Formalin, scientifically known as methanal, is an aldehyde and the simplest of all aldehydes. Acetone, scientifically known as propanone, is a ketone and the simplest of all ketones.

One of the most useful manners by which to classify the standard (or common) amino acids is based on the polarity (that is, the distribution of electric charge) of the R group (e.g., side chain).

Group I amino acids are glycine, alanine, valine, leucine, isoleucine, proline, phenylalanine, methionine, and tryptophan. The R groups of these amino acids have either aliphatic or aromatic groups.
