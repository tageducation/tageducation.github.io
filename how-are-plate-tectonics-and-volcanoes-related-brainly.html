6. 4. How will you relate the distribution of volcanoes with plate boundaries?A volcanoes occur at the center of tectonic platesB. volcanoes and plate boundaries are not relatedC. volcanoes occur in any part of tectonic platesD. volcanoes occur where two plates intersected​


Answer:

Mountains, like volcanos, don’t just decide to rise up from the plains. All mountain orogeny starts with forces that can only be addressed by movement or displacement of the material being forced.

The continental plates are all formed from the lightest and least dense material on Earth. If their material wasn’t the lightest or least dense, it wouldn’t be on a continental plate - it would most likely be on an oceanic plate or in the mantle itself.

When two continental plates make contact, and the forces that drove them to meet each other are still pushing, several things can happen, and is addressed in one of four ways, listed here:

One plate dives under the other (true if one of the plates is oceanic crustal plate diving under a continental plate, causing lots of earthquakes and spawning many volcanos) - if both plates are truly continental plates, they won’t be subducting, so this option won’t apply;

OR Both plates stop and slowly grind their way “around” the other (true if both of these plates meet in California), and of course extra resistance in grinding past each other has raised the Transverse Mountains (Santa Monica, San Bernardino ranges) the Sierra Nevada and the Coastal Ranges of California, and partly responsible for the Siskiyou mountains and the Cascade Range;

OR Any continental crustal elements that are sitting on the colliding plates (even is one is subducting) and get “scraped off” onto the continental plate edge and build up into mountains (true in the Aleutian island chain (and other places));

OR Both plates are still pushing, are of equal material density (and therefore will not subduct) so the only way to go is up, producing one or more mountains and / or mountain ranges (e.g. Himalayas, Rockies, Appalachians, Sierra Nevada, Coastal Ranges (Pacific coast of US), Alps, Andes, Great Dividing Range (AUS), Tien Shan, Karakorum, Atlas (Morocco).

In order to be pushed upwards, the material must break so it can be folded. Each break is called a fault (can be a minor fault or a major fault) and each movement by the material along these faults will cause an earthquake (from tiny to massive).

Where the mountain ranges are still growing, there will be much movement, and many earthquakes - where the mountain range is also above a subduction zone, we will add volcanos to the mix.

10. How are the locations of volcanoes related to plate tectonic?​


Answer:

Most of the world's volcanoes are found around the edges of tectonic plates, both on land and in the oceans.

On land, volcanoes form when one tectonic plate moves under another. Usually a thin, heavy oceanic plate subducts, or moves under, a thicker continental plate. When this happens, the ocean plate sinks into the mantle.
