24. Evaluate the following advertisements. Identify what type of fallacies are used in these advertisements and EXPLAIN WHY.


Type of Fallacies in Advertising

Advertising fallacies are logical mistakes that advertising utilizes to try and convince customers to buy a product or service. Advertisers may claim that their product or service helps their consumers in order to persuade viewers to purchase it.

14 Fallacies in Advertising

Ad hominem – Appeals by casting doubt on a competitor's trustworthiness.  This type of fallacy seeks to defame a person or persuade others to doubt their credibility, reliability, or personality.Appeal to emotions – Focus on generating a certain emotion in the customer, even if that emotion has no reasonable explanation. This is a frequent marketing strategy, and advertisers may introduce it with other fallacies in a marketing campaign.False dilemma – Incorrectly restricts the number of options available to customers and encourages them to pick from the limited options. Advertisers may use an "either-or" argument to convey a false dilemma.Appeal to the people – This fallacy claims that something is real because the majority of people believe it. This appeal in advertising implies that because most people believe something is real, the customer should likewise believe it.Scare tactic – Fear is a sort of emotional appeal that is used to persuade clients to buy a product or service. This strategy frequently offers a situation in which a threat endangers something that most consumers care deeply about.False cause – Advertisers may employ this fallacy to suggest that their product produces a favorable result for a customer when, in fact, it is the setting or context in which the customer utilizes the product that produces the positive result.Hasty generalization – A hasty generalization is based on an insufficient amount of information. This sort of mistake can lead to statements based purely on hearsay.Red herring – The presentation of irrelevant information that confuses the customers from the main point of an issue. Advertisers may use this approach to defame an opponent by stressing a problem or issue unrelated to a product's or service's function or usefulness.Traditional wisdom – This fallacy is based on the belief that if something was true in the past, it must therefore be true in the present. This fallacy in advertising might play to a customer's feeling of nostalgia and history, or to their notion that doing something consistently for many years or generations indicates its validity.Appeal to popularity – A bandwagon fallacy, as it is often known, claims that customers should buy a product or service because everyone else does.Halo effect – Advertisers apply this fallacy to persuade customers to buy other products or services following the success of an unrelated product or service.Slippery slope – A slippery slope fallacy states that if the result of a series of events is undesirable or negative, then the initial event and concept for its origin were equally bad.Distribution fallacy – It gives the features of the whole to its parts. Customers may think that if a brand has a positive reputation, it maintains that reputation in all aspects of its brand.Division fallacy – They may extend the reputation of a certain product or department within a company to the overall reputation of the company.
