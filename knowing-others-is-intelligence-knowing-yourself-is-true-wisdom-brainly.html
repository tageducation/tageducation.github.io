The first quote is from Lao Tzu. The second quote is from J Paulsen. Both of the quotes teach us how we should live our lives being contented with what we have. Not aiming for material things which are only temporary.

About the first quote:

                              "Knowing others is intelligence,
                                      knowing yourself is true wisdom.
                              Mastering others is strength,
                                     mastering yourself is true power"

These lines explain that it takes intelligence and strength to know other people especially those who are around you. It also gives us an understanding that if we want to live a life we wanted, we need first to understand what we want from within.

Other individuals are so drawn to be updated and to keep up with the trend but deep inside, they are not happy. This is because they thought that happiness is dependable on other individuals but in fact, it should come from within. We should all understand that happiness cannot be fulfilled if we are not authentically happy from the inside.

                               "If you realize that you have enough,
                                        you are truly rich."

The line explains that in order to be happy, we should be satisfied with what we have. We should not aim for the things that will temporarily give us happiness because these all will just pass by and fade.

About the second quote:

                                "the cost of not following your heart,

                                        is spending the rest of your life

                                             wishing you had."

This quote by J Paulsen gives us an understanding that we should follow what our hearts says. If we allow ourselves to be manipulated by others about the things we want, we will not be happy within. Who is Lao Tzu?

Lao Tzu is a Chinese phrase that translates to "Old Sage" or "Old Master," as well as the name of a famous philosopher and thinker who lived between 614 and 517 BC. The ‘Tao Te Ching' is often thought to have been composed by him.

Although the author of the Tao is (officially) unknown, it is commonly assigned to the Chinese prophet and putative founder of Taoism, Lao Tzu — it is clear from reading the Tao that it is a compilation of wisdom handed down for generations and recorded down at some point
