Answer:

Water Cycle

Earth is a truly unique in its abundance of water. Water is necessary to sustaining life on Earth, and helps tie together the Earth's lands, oceans, and atmosphere into an integrated system. Precipitation, evaporation, freezing and melting and condensation are all part of the hydrological cycle - a never-ending global process of water circulation from clouds to land, to the ocean, and back to the clouds. This cycling of water is major physical components of the global water cycle include the evaporation from the ocean and land surfaces, the transport of water vapor by the atmosphere, precipitation onto the ocean and land surfaces, the net atmospheric transport of water from land areas to ocean, and the return flow of fresh water from the land back into the ocean. The additional components of oceanic water transport are few, including the mixing of fresh water through the oceanic boundary layer, transport by ocean currents, and sea ice processes. On land the situation is considerably more complex, and includes the deposition of rain and snow on land; water flow in runoff; infiltration of water into the soil and groundwater; storage of water in soil, lakes and streams, and groundwater; polar and glacial ice; and use of water in vegetation and human activities. Illustration of the water cycle showing the ocean, land, mountains, and rivers returning to the ocean. Processes labeled include: precipitation, condensation, evaporation, evaportranspiration (from tree into atmosphere), radiative exchange, surface runoff, ground water and stream flow, infiltration, percolation and soil moisture.

Hydrological cycle

The hydrologic cycle describes the pilgrimage of water as water molecules make their way from the Earth's surface to the atmosphere, and back again. This gigantic system, powered by energy from the sun, is a continuous exchange of moisture between the oceans, the atmosphere, and the land. Credit: NASA GSFC Water and Energy Cycle web site. This diagram show the relationship between Physical Oceanography, Biological Oceanography, and Water Cycle. Feedbacks between Physical Oceanography and Water Cycle are Evaporation minus Precipitation and Fresh water transports (i.e. Goldsborough Circulation). Biological in the ocean is affected by the water cycle via the Mixed Layer Depth and Run off from land. Finally, feedback between Physical and Biological Oceanography include the sea-ice and haline enivironments.

Evaporation ("E") controls the loss of fresh water and precipitation ("P") governs most of the gain of fresh water. Scientists monitor the relationship between these two primary processes in the oceans. Inputs from rivers and melting ice can also contribute to fresh water gains. Evaporation minus precipitation is usually referred to as the net flux of fresh water or the total fresh water in or out of the oceans. E-P determines surface salinity of the ocean, which helps determine the stability of the water column. Salinity and temperature determine the density of ocean water, and density influences the circulation. E-P determines surface salinity of the ocean, which helps determine the stability of the water column. Precipitation also affects the height of the ocean surface indirectly via salinity and density.

Water Pyramid

At the surface of the ocean, mixing by the wind can change the density to the point where the temperature and salinity are constant throughout the mixed layer. The depth of the mixed layer is called the mixed layer depth. Phytoplankton in the ocean can impact this mixed layer depth by absorbing the sun's heat at the surface thus decreasing the amount of sunlight that reaches deeper water. In effect, the phytoplankton causes the heating to be confined to a thin layer near the surface and reduces the thickness of the mixed layer.

The ocean surface is constantly being stirred up by wind and changes in density or buoyancy. The ocean naturally has different physical characteristics with depth. As depth increases, temperature decreases because the sun only heats surface waters. Warm water is lighter or more buoyant than cold water, so the warm surface water stays near the surface. However, surface water is also subject to evaporation. When seawater evaporates, water is removed, salt remains, and relatively salty water is left behind. This relatively salty water can float at the surface; for example, in the tropics it floats because is it so warm and buoyant.

21. list down the effect of water cycle on living things


Answer:

The water cycle is an extremely important process because it enables the availability of water for all living organisms and regulates weather patterns on our planet. If water didn’t naturally recycle itself, we would run out of clean water, which is essential to life.

Freshwater seems abundant, but when accounting for all the water on Earth, it's in limited supply. Just three percent of the water on our planet is freshwater. A majority of this water, about two percent of the world total, is contained in glaciers and ice sheets or stored below ground. The remaining one percent is found in lakes, rivers and wetland areas or transported through the atmosphere in the form of water vapor, clouds and precipitation. Rain and snowfall replenish freshwater sources, making it vital to know when, where and how much water is falling at any given time. Using NASA's Global Precipitation Measurement satellite, researchers can track precipitation worldwide and monitor levels from space.

Plants wouldn't grow without precipitation (and thus anything consuming the plants wouldn't survive and so forth). Infiltration of water filters and cleans our water. Glaciers, ice, and snow can act as stores of freshwater for both humans and other organisms.

Water also moves through the living organisms in an ecosystem. Plants soak up large amounts of water through their roots. The water then moves up the plant and evaporates from the leaves in a process called transpiration. The process of transpiration, like evaporation, returns water back into the atmosphere.

When animals breathe, their warm lungs fill with air. Inside the lungs, some of that air condenses into water vapor. When an animal exhales, they release more water vapor than they breathed in, which adds to the water present in the water cycle. Many animals also sweat to cool down.

Water is also constantly moving through another cycle -- the human water cycle, that powers our homes, hydrates our bodies, irrigates our crops and processes our waste. The tight connection between water, food and energy makes them dependent on one another.

What is the importance of the Water Cycle?

The water cycle is an enormously important part of life because it ensures water availability for all living organisms and controls the weather patterns on our planet. If it did not naturally recycle, we would run out of clean water, which is fundamental to life, if it did not naturally recycle. The majority of this water, approximately 2% of the total, is held in glaciers and ice sheets or buried underground. One percent is seen in lakes, rivers, and wetland areas, or it is transferred through the atmosphere as water vapor, clouds, and precipitation.

Rain and snowfall replenish freshwater supplies. Therefore it is essential to know when, where, and how much precipitation is falling at any one time. The movement of water is constant. A few days before today's precipitation, it may have been ocean water. And the water you witness in a river or stream could have been snow on a mountain peak. Water can be seen in the atmosphere, on land, in the ocean, and underground. It travels from place to place via the water cycle, altering as the climate changes.

How do Water cycles affect climate change?

As higher global temperatures increase the rate of evaporation worldwide, it seems likely that climate change is hastening portions of the water cycle. On average, more precipitation results from more evaporation. As the climate warms, we are already observing the effects of increased evaporation and precipitation rates, and these effects are predicted to intensify over the next century.

Greater evaporation and precipitation rates are not uniformly distributed around the earth. As the typical rain belts and desert locations vary in reaction to a changing climate, certain regions may see heavier-than-normal precipitation. In contrast, others may become prone to droughts.

Bad Effects:

More rain and flooding. As a result of increased evaporation, more water is in the air. Therefore storms can deliver more extreme rainfall events in some regions.Warmer temperatures result in increased evaporation, transforming water into vapor in the air and causing drought in some world areas.Warmer ocean surface waters can cause hurricanes and tropical storms to become more drastic.Heat waves may have become more frequent in a more significant number of world regions.

Good Effects:

Humans cannot survive and stay without water for more than a week. Water comprises 60 to 70 percent of all living stuff.The hydrologic cycle, sometimes known as the water cycle, distributes fresh water throughout the world's surface.Humans, animals, and plants are continuously supplied with fresh water through the water cycle.
