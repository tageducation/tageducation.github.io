27. what is protective structure of cattle​


The protective structure of cattle is the skin of cattle (cowhide) and its fur.

Cattle, like all mamals, have hard bone skeletons made of calcium phosphate (Ca10(PO4)6(OH)2; They all have thick, protective skin that lacks scales. All mammals have hair, however the quantity of hair varies widely amongst mammal families. Cetaceans and sirenians have essentially no hair, but marine carnivorans such as pinnipeds, polar bears, sea otters, and marine otters have dense fur.

The protective structure of cattle is skin and fur. Fur is a protein filament that develops from the skin. Hair is commonly referred to as fur in non-human animals. All mammals have hair on their bodies, however the amount varies widely across species. Hair has a number of roles in cattle. Fur may offer heat insulation and keep cattle comfortable in cold climes. Touch and movement can also be detected by nerve receptors located at the base of hair follicles. Hair color patterns can also be used to disguise cattle for defense.

Cowhide is a cattle's natural, unbleached skin and hair. It keeps the animal's original coloration. Cowhide is a layer of generally soft, flexible exterior tissue that covers the body of a bovine and has three purposes: protection, control, and feeling. Other animal coverings, such as the exoskeleton of an arthropod, have a distinct developmental origin, structure, and chemical makeup.

In addition, cattle (and other ruminants) have the most specialized adaptation: a huge four-chambered stomach that serves as a fermentation tank. Bacteria and other microbes breakdown tough plant fibers inside the rumen, the stomach's biggest chamber (cellulose).
