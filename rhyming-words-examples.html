Answer:

A rhyme is a repetition of similar sounds (usually, exactly the same sound) in the final stressed syllables and any following syllables of two or more words. In other words, rhyming words are two or more words that don’t start with the same sound, but they end with the same sound (maybe with the same letters). Some examples of rhyming words are: cat, fat, bad, ad, add, sad, etc.

Poems and songs often use rhyming to create a rhythm or a repeated pattern of sound, and sometimes poems will also tell a story. By learning these words, students will increase their sentence building and creative writing skills.

Things to remember about the difference between rhyming words and homophones:

Rhyming words have different beginning sounds, but have the same vowel and ending sounds, for example: cat/sat/pat/hat, spin/fin/chin/pin, etc. Homophones are words that sound alike but have different meanings and spellings, for example: pain/pane, hi/high/, you/yew/ewe, there/their/they’re, to/too/two.

20. Read the poem "The Seven Ages of Man". Make a list of the rhyming words, and determine which are examples of internal rhyme and end rhyme.


By now, you will have already know what a rhyme is but to understand what we are looking for in William Shakespeare's The Seven Ages of Man, we must find out what internal rhymes and end rhymes mean. After the definition, I will list each example taken from the poem itself. I hope this helps!

Rhymes are often used in poems to give it a musical quality and to create a pattern that helps with memorization. Rhymes are often arranged in pleasing auditory flow. There are two ways that rhymes are arranged in poems.

End Rhyme

This is the pattern we are most familiar with. End rhymes are two rhyming words placed at the end of every or every other line or sentences. This creates an emphasis on the two words, usually a correlation or an opposition.

End Rhymes from The Seven Ages of Man

And then the whining schoolboy, with his satchel

And shining morning face, creeping like snail

...And then the lover,

...Then a soldier.

Even in the cannon’s mouth. And then the justice,

In fair round belly with good capon lined,

With eyes severe and beard of formal cut,

Full of wise saws and modern instances;

With spectacles on nose and pouch on side;

His youthful hose, well saved, a world too wide

Is second childishness and mere oblivion,

Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything.

Welcome. Set down your venerable burthen

Internal Rhyme

The placement of the rhyming words are within the lines and not at the end, this is why they are also called middle rhymes. It some instances internal rhymes are placed in two separate lines as long as they are not at the end.

Internal Rhymes from The Seven Ages of Man

And all the men and women merely players

Mewling and puking in the nurse’s arms

And then the whining schoolboy, with his satchel

And shining morning face, creeping like snail

With spectacles on nose and pouch on side;

His youthful hose, well saved, a world too wide

For his shrunk shank; and his big manly voice

Combination of End Rhyme and Internal Rhyme

All the world’s a stage,  → end rhyme

His acts being seven age(s). At first the infant,  → internal rhyme

Made to his mistress’ eyebrow. Then a soldier,    → end rhyme

Full of strange oaths, and bearded like the pard,

Jealous in honor, sudden and quick in quarrel,   → internal rhyme

24. Give examples of slant rhyme and perfect rhyme​


Explanation:

Slant rhyme,
also known as near rhyme or half rhyme, is a type of rhyme where the ending consonant sounds of two words are similar but not identical. Some examples of slant rhyme include 'room' and 'storm', 'tree' and 'dry', 'grieve' and 'leave', 'love' and 'move', and 'rang' and 'bring'. In contrast, perfect rhyme is a type of rhyme where the ending sounds of two words are identical. Examples of perfect rhyme include 'cat' and 'hat', 'sing' and 'bring', 'chair' and 'hair', 'day' and 'play', and 'heart' and 'start'."

Perfect rhyme

is a type of rhyme where the ending sounds of two words are identical. Some examples of perfect rhyme include 'cat' and 'hat', 'sing' and 'bring', 'chair' and 'hair', 'day' and 'play', and 'heart' and 'start'.

End rhyme is defined as “when a poem has lines ending with words that sound the same.” End rhyme is also called tail rhyme or terminal rhyme. It is one of many types of rhyme. Two or more lines of the poem have to rhyme for it to be considered end rhyme, but they don't have to be consecutive lines.

Internal rhyme, or middle rhyme, is rhyme that occurs within a single line of verse, or between internal phrases across multiple lines. By contrast, rhyme between line endings is known as end rhyme.
