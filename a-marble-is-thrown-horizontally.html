11. a marble is thrown horizontally from a table top with a vilocty of 2.0 m/s. the marble falls 1.25 m away from the table edge. how high is the table?​


Quantities Used:

vi = initial velocity

t = time of flight

θ = angle of release

dx = horizontal distance

vix = horizontal component of initial velocity

ax = horizontal acceleration = 0

dy = vertical distance

viy = vertical component of initial velocity

ay = vertical acceleration = g = 9.8 m/s²

Given Values:

vi = 2.0 m/s

dx = 1.25 m

Solution:Solving for t

Note: ax = 0 and θ = 0° (thrown horizontally)

dx = (vix)(t)+ (1/2)(ax)(t²)

dx = (vi cos θ)(t) + (1/2)(0)(t²)

dx = (vi cos 0°)(t) + 0

dx = vi(1)(t)

dx = (vi)(t)

t = dx / vi

t = 1.25 m / 2.0 m/s

t = 0.625 s

Solving for dy

Note: ay = 9.8 m/s² and θ = 0° (thrown horizontally)

dy = (viy)(t) + (1/2)(ay)(t²)

dy =(vi sin θ)(t) + (1/2)(ay)(t²)

dy = (2.0 m/s)(sin 0°)(0.625 s) + (1/2)(9.8 m/s²)(0.625 s)²

dy = 0 + 1.91 m

dy = 1.91 m

Therefore, the height of the table is 1.91 m.

12. Given two marbles, released at the same time, Marble A is dropped while Marble B is thrown horizontally coming from the same height. Which one do you think would reach the ground first? Why?


Given two marbles, released at the same time, Marble A is dropped while Marble B is thrown horizontally coming from the same height. Which one do you think would reach the ground first? why?

Answer:

The answer in the real world is that marble A will reach the ground first, assuming that the marbles are identical at least.

The reason for this is that drag deceleration on a marble due to air resistance is proportional to greater than the marble speed. In fact it’s very close to being proportional to the speed squared. The effect of this is that the additional horizontal speed causes an increase in drag which also adds to the vertical deceleration of the marble.

To see this let’s suppose for the sake of discussion that the drag deceleration on our marbles equals 0.1v^2 for marble speed v . Consider a point in marble A’s flight where it is travelling downwards at 3 m/s. So the drag deceleration is 0.1×32=0.9 m/s^2. Now consider marble B at a point where it is also travelling downwards at 3 m/s, and let’s say that the horizontal speed at this point is 4 m/s. So the combined speed is 5 m/s giving a drag deceleration against the direction of travel of 0.1×5^2=2.5 m/s^2 . The downward component of the drag deceleration is 3/5 ths of this, equal to 1.5 m/s^2, which is considerably larger than the 0.9 m/s^2 deceleration for marble A falling at the same speed.

So marble B accelerates downwards due to gravity more slowly than marble A due to B’s additional horizontal speed, and therefore marble B takes significantly longer to reach the ground. Marbles being very small objects experience significant drag and so this effect is easily measurable. A 1 cm diameter glass marble dropped from a height of 20m would take around 2.14secs. to hit the ground, whereas the same marble thrown horizontally at 20 m/s will take around 2.25 secs.

25. A marble is thrown horizontally from a table top with a velocity of 1.50m/s. The marble fal 0.70m from the table edge. What is the marlble's velocity bjust before it hits the ground it hits the ground?


Background

Because the marble moved both horizontally (farther from the table) and vertically (closer to the ground), we say that we are dealing with a trajectory, because the marble’s motion is also influenced by gravity. Other examples of objects moving in a trajectory would be:

Analyze

As shown in Figure 1, we are given two values: the initial horizontal velocity of the marble (1.50 m/s) and its distance from the table after falling to the ground (0.70 m). What we are asked is the velocity of the marble after it hits the ground, which is its final velocity.

However, we know that the marble moves in two dimensions: horizontal and vertical. Therefore, we not only have velocity in the horizontal direction, but also in the vertical direction (see Figure 2). As a result, the marble can be thought of as moving:

Horizontally, at a constant [tex]1.50\text{ m/s}[/tex]Vertically, from rest, and speeding up by [tex]9.81\text{ m/s}^2[/tex] because of gravityAssumption

Solve

Let's list down all our knowns and unknowns. We denote them as the following:

Initial horizontal velocity of the marble: [tex]v_{0x} = 1.50 m/s[/tex]Initial vertical velocity of the marble: [tex]v_{0y} = 0[/tex]Vertical acceleration due to gravity: [tex]g = 9.81 m/s^2[/tex]Horizontal distance of marble from the table: [tex]d = 0.70 m[/tex]Final velocity of the marble: [tex]v_f = ?[/tex]

To solve the problem, we break the problem down to three smaller steps.

Time it took for the marble to reach the ground

This will be easy because we are given the initial horizontal velocity (which does not change) and the horizontal distance travelled by the marble. This represents the horizontal component of our trajectory.

[tex]d=v_{0x}t\\0.70\text{ m} = (1.50\text{ m/s})t\\t=0.4667\text{ s}[/tex]

It took the marble 0.4667 s to reach the ground.

Final vertical velocity of the marble

It will also take 0.4667 s for the marble to reach the ground if we were to drop it from the height of the table. This represents the vertical component of our trajectory.

[tex]v_{fy}=v_{0y}+gt\\v_{fy}=0+(-9.81\text{ m/s}^2)(0.4667\text{ s})\\v_{fy}=-4.5783\text{ m/s}[/tex]

The final vertical velocity [tex]v_{fy}[/tex] of the marble was -4.578 m/s, which implies that the velocity is downwards.

Final velocity of the marble

The question did not state whether it's the horizontal or vertical velocity that's needed. Therefore, we should assume that the question asks for the vector sum of the horizontal and vertical velocities. (Please see Figure 3.) The magnitude of the final velocity can be calculated as:

[tex]v_f = \sqrt{v_{fx}^2+y_{fy}^2}[/tex]

The horizontal velocity does not change, so [tex]v_{fx}=v_{0x}=1.50\text{ m/s}[/tex]. Hence,

[tex]v_f = \sqrt{(1.50\text{ m/s})^2+(-4.5783\text{ m/s})^2}\\v_f = \sqrt{23.21083\text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2}}\\v_f=4.8178\text{ m/s}[/tex]

The final speed of the marble just before it hits the ground is therefore around 4.82 m/s.

Meanwhile, the direction of the final speed can be calculated by noting that [tex]v_{fx}[/tex] and [tex]v_{fy}[/tex] form a right triangle (explained here https://brainly.ph/question/7960). Therefore, the direction, represented by [tex]\theta[/tex], can be calculated using the arctangent:

[tex]\theta =\text{tan}^{-1}(v_{fy}/v_{fx})\\\theta =\text{tan}^{-1}(-4.578/1.50)\\\theta=-71.86^{\text{o}}[/tex]

Therefore, the velocity of the marble just before it hits the ground is 4.82 m/s, -71.86° from the horizontal.
