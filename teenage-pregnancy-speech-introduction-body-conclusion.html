Answer:

Few teens (ages 15 to 19) on birth control use the most effective types.

Use of Long-Acting Reversible Contraception (LARC) is low.

1. Less than 5% of teens on birth control use LARC.

2. Most teens use birth control pills and condoms, methods which are less effective at preventing pregnancy when not used properly.

3. There are several barriers for teens who might consider LARC:

Many teens know very little about LARC. Some teens mistakenly think they cannot use LARC because of their age.

4. Clinics also report barriers:

High upfront costs for supplies. Providers may lack awareness about the safety and effectiveness of LARC for teens. Providers may lack training on insertion and removal.

What can be done

Parents, guardians, and caregivers can

1. Talk with their teens about sex, including:

encouraging them not to have sex. encouraging them to use effective birth control to prevent pregnancy, along with condoms to protect against sexually transmitted diseases.

2. Visit a health care provider with the teen to learn about various types of birth control, including LARC.

3. Check with their health plan(s) about coverage of preventive services. Birth control and counseling may be available for teens at no out-of-pocket cost.

Teens can

Choose not to have sex. Talk openly to parents or other trusted adults and ask how they can get birth control if they choose to be sexually active. 30. introduction:teenage pregnancy​


Answer:

what is eenage pregnancy?

Teenage pregnancy is a public health issue that affects millions of young people, particularly in the United States. It is associated with numerous negative outcomes, including higher rates of high school dropout, poverty, and poor health outcomes for both the mother and child. It is also associated with greater risk of physical and sexual abuse. To address the issue, it is important to provide comprehensive sex education, access to contraception, and support services to teens who become pregnant. It is also important to focus on improving social, economic, and educational opportunities for teens, as this can reduce the risk of teenage pregnancy.

How to prevent teenage pregnancy:

1. Educate teens about sex: Make sure teens have access to comprehensive sex education that covers topics such as contraception, sexually transmitted infections, and healthy relationships.

2. Encourage communication: Help teens develop the skills to communicate openly and honestly with their parents and other trusted adults about sex and relationships.

3. Delay sex: Encourage teens to delay having sex until they are emotionally ready.

4. Make contraception available: Make sure teens have access to contraception and other forms of birth control if they choose to have sex.

5. Provide support: Make sure teens have access to support and services if they do become pregnant.

6. Talk about values: Encourage teens to think about their values and consider the consequences of their decisions.

7. Foster healthy relationships: Help teens build healthy relationships that are based on respect and trust.

8. Set a good example: Model healthy relationship behaviors and talk to teens about why it is important to wait until they are ready to have sex.
