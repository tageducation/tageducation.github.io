What is the most special gift you've ever received from your parents?

When I was 10, my parents gave me my own room.

Perhaps this doesn’t sound very special. But it was. Thing is – we only had that one bedroom.

Prior to this day, we all shared a sleeping space. We had two rooms total – one tiny bedroom and another room, barely bigger, which served as the living room/dining room/kitchen. Our apartment was easy to claim off the waiting list – it was a university residence so tiny that nobody wanted it.

However, it was a step up for us. Our apartment back home in Russia was a studio room with a miniscule half-kitchen attached to it. Our new Californian home had mold and thin walls, but for us, it would be just fine.

I don’t know if it was a special occasion. On that day I returned from school and saw my parents’ sleeping couch in the living room. Confused, I went into our bedroom and saw that it had been transformed. Piled in the corner were my mattresses. On a bookshelf were my books, my toys. The walls had my drawings. There was even a small purple desk with art supplies, some of which looked new. I glanced at my mom, completely befuddled.

“I thought it was time for you to have your own space,” she said.

“But where will you sleep?”

“We’ll be fine on the couch outside. I want you to have a place to call your own.”

I’d never heard of something like this before. I knew of children who slept on couches in living rooms, because there was only one bedroom and the parents slept in it. I knew of families which had houses, so everyone had their own room. But I’d be the only child I knew who had her own room, while my parents slept on a couch outside. Wasn’t it more important for them to have their space? They were adults. I was just a kid. This felt wrong.

But my mom insisted and I had my own room. I had a space to be safe. I had a space to retreat to. I had a space in which I could read, draw, and dream. I never knew what it meant to have ownership of a space before, so it was hard for me to understand what my parents gave up for me. But as time went on, it dawned more and more on me that the room was just the tip of the iceberg. I had the kind of parents who silently, relentlessly sacrificed for me. They gave up their space in the world to carve one out for me. They left everything they knew to come to a new country. They put aside their own exhaustion, frustration, and fear to guide me through childhood.

When I was 10, my parents gave up their right to a safe, personal space, so that I could have one. Now that I’m 26, I finally understand how deeply special that gift was.
