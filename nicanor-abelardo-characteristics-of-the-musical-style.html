Characteristics of Musical Styles of Nicanor Abelardo

Nicanor Abelardo is a Filipino composer popular for his many Kundiman originals. The characteristics of musicals styles of Nicanor Abelardo is mainly based on Filipino Kundiman. In fact, he is one of the Filipino musicians who revolutionized Kundiman songs into their sophisticated art forms that we know of today. Kundiman is a genre of traditional Filipino music that is mostly, if not all, love song. Furthermore, Kundiman is characterized by its smooth flow and gentle rhythm with a 3/4 time signature.

16. What is Nicanor abelardo musical background


           Nicanor Abelardo was five years old when he was introduced to music once his father taught him solfeggio, and how to play the bandurria and guitar at 6 years old. His rapid instrument mastery has made him a prodigy in their city. He was able to play Rossini's "William Tell Overture" arrangement of his father on guitar at age 6. He also played his father's guitar and other string instruments and learned to play quickly without much trouble. In 1901, he wrote and dedicated his first composition to his aunt, "Ang Unang Buko."

Abelardo joined the newly established Conservatory of Music of the University of the Philippines in 1916, taking courses under Guy F. Harrison and Robert Schofield. He composed the melody of the official anthem of the university, U.P., during his studies. In 1917, It was officially entitled, U.P Naming Mahal.

              His musical talent was immediately noticed by the Conservatory, and by 1918 he was appointed assistant professor in solfeggio and harmony. He took piano lessons under Jose Estella, Bonifacio Abdon's violin, and Victorino Carrion's voice. During this time, he started to lead movie orchestras and play the piano in saloons and cabarets, and this is also the time he began to drink alcohol. He finally graduated in 1921 with a degree in Science and Composition, followed by a postgraduate course that he completed the following year.

             When teaching at the conservatory he published most of his important works. His graduation work, Piano Sonata in G Major, was the first sonata of a Philippine composer and his postgraduate piece, Piano Concerto in B-flat major, was the first concerto of a Philippine composer. During his time, almost all of his kundiman was written as well.

             He was awarded a grant in 1931 to pursue advanced study in music abroad. Despite his small savings, he accepted the offer and saw his promising prospects in the future.
