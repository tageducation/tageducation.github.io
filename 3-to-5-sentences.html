14. For items 3-5, identify the parts of the paragraph. 3. The Beginning Sentence/Introduction 4. The Middle Sentences/Body 5. The Ending Sentence/Conclusion


Answer:

3.An introduction is the first paragraph of your paper. The goal of your introduction is to let your reader know the topic of the paper and what points will be made about the topic.


4.Body paragraphs are units of text that offer supporting evidence to back up the thesis statement of an essay, report, or story. A good body paragraph contains three main sections: a topic sentence (or key sentence), relevant supporting sentences, and a closing (or transition) sentence.

5.A concluding sentence indicates that you are bringing closure to a paragraph. For each paragraph, the reader should be able to identify what your key points are, based on the concluding sentence. It should not include any information that was not discussed in the paragraph.

Second Conditional Sentences

1. If I won the lottery, I would buy a big house.

2. If I met the Queen of England, I would say hello.

3. She would travel all over the world if she were rich.

4. If I had his number, I would call him.

5. If I were you, I wouldn't go out with that man.

Third Conditional Sentences

1. If my alarm had gone off, I wouldn’t have been late to work.

2. If there hadn’t been so much traffic we wouldn’t have missed our flight.

3. If he had studied harder, he would have passed the exam.

4. If it hadn’t been snowing heavily, we’d have carried on skiing.

5. The company would have survived if there hadn’t been a recession.

Explanation:

SECOND CONDITIONAL SENTENCES has two uses

First, we can use it to talk about things in the future that are probably not going to be true.

Second, we can use it to talk about something in the present which is impossible, because it's not true.

THIRD CONDITIONAL SENTENCES

To make a sentence in the third conditional, we use,

If + past perfect, would/wouldn’t have + past participle.

As with all conditionals, you can also invert this structure:

Would have + past participle if + past perfect.

The word would is often contracted to ‘d by native speakers. It’s also acceptable to use this in informal writing. And in speech it’s common to contract have to ‘ve in the third conditional.

However, we can’t write this, even in an informal context. It’s useful to be aware of it though, so you can identify it when native speakers say it.

As an alternative to would, we can complete the second part of a third conditional sentence with could.
