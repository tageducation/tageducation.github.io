Examples of bias:

1. Racial bias: Prejudice or discrimination based on a person's race or ethnicity.

2. Gender bias: Prejudice or discrimination based on a person's gender or gender identity.

3. Age bias: Prejudice or discrimination based on a person's age.

4. Religious bias: Prejudice or discrimination based on a person's religious beliefs or practices.

5. Sexual orientation bias: Prejudice or discrimination based on a person's sexual orientation.

Examples of not-bias:

1. Factual reporting: Presenting information in a neutral and objective manner, without personal opinions or biases influencing the content.

2. Impartial decision-making: Making decisions based on facts and evidence, rather than personal preferences or biases.

3. Fair treatment: Treating all individuals equally and without prejudice, regardless of their personal characteristics or circumstances.

4. Balanced perspectives: Considering multiple viewpoints and perspectives when making decisions or forming opinions.

5. Objective evaluation: Assessing something based on objective criteria, rather than personal biases or preferences.

22. what is bias and non bias​


Answer:

Bias refers to a tendency or inclination, especially one that is preconceived or unreasoned. Bias can be positive or negative, and it can influence how a person thinks, feels, or acts. Bias can also affect the way that a person perceives and interprets information.

Non-bias, on the other hand, refers to a lack of bias or fairness. It means that a person is not influenced by their personal feelings or opinions, and that they are able to make decisions objectively, based on facts and evidence. Non-bias is often associated with fairness and impartiality, and it is an important principle in many areas, including research, journalism, and the legal system.

Answer:

What is bias by omission?

Bias by omission – leaving one side out of an article, or a series of articles over a period of time; ignoring facts that tend to disprove liberal or conservative claims.

What is bias by the headline?

Bias by headline

Headlines are the must-read part of a news story because they are often printed in large and bold fonts.  Headlines can be misleading, conveying excitement when the story is not exciting, expressing approval or disapproval.  These two headlines describe the same event.

What is bias by the use of language?

Biased language includes expressions that demean or exclude people because of age, sex, race, ethnicity, social class, or physical or mental traits.”

What is bias by repetition?

Repetition bias is a cognitive bias in which there is a willingness to believe what we have been told most often and by the greatest number of different sources.

What is bias by media?

Media bias is the bias of journalists and news producers within the mass media in the selection of many events and stories that are reported and how they are covered. ... The direction and degree of media bias in various countries are widely disputed.

What is bias through the use of names of titles?

Bias through the use of names and titles

News media often use labels and titles to describe people, places, and events. In many places around the world, one person's friend is another person's enemy.  A person can be called an "ex-con" or be referred to as someone who "served time for a drug charge".
