Example:

Move the decimal place to the left to create a new number from 1 up to 10.

Where’s the decimal point in 312000000000? Because it’s a whole number the decimal point is understood to be at the end of the number 312000000000.

Move the decimal place to the left to create a new number from 1 up to 10.

Determine the exponent which is the number of times you moved the decimal.

In this example you moved the decimal 11 times also because you moved the decimal to the left the exponent is positive. Therefore a = 11 and so you get

10^11

( N × 10 )

To see an exponent that’s negative write

.00000031 in scientific notation.

Move the decimal place to the right to create a new number from 1 up to 10.

So N = 3.1

Determine the exponent, which is the number of times you moved the decimal.

In this example you moved the decimal 7 times also because you moved the decimal to the right, the exponent is negative. Therefore a = –7 and so you get

10 ^ -7

Put the number in the correct form for scientific notation

N ( × 10 )

.00000031 = 3.1 × 10 ^ - 7

4. write the following in scientific notation​


Answer:

Scientific Notation

Here are the answers:

275000 = 2.75 × 10^5 0.00063 = 6.3 × 10^-4 9000 = 9 × 10^3 800 = 8 × 10^2 0.00038 = 3.8 × 10^-4 86500 = 8.65 × 10^4 4000000 = 4 × 10^6 5.75 = 5.75 × 10^0 0.000026 = 2.6 × 10^-5 45 = 4.5 × 10^1

Step-by-step explanation:

When converting a number into its scientific notation, you have to remember these steps:

If you are converting numbers from 1 and above:

First, identify the digit on the left most side of the number. Copy it. Then, write a decimal point after the first digit that you copied. Then, write all of the remaining digits. Do not copy zeroes. You only need to copy zeroes if the last zero is followed by a decimal point, or if the last zero is followed by a whole number. Then, write “x”, followed by the number “10” Then, write the exponent after 10. The exponent is equal to all of the digits (including zero) after the left most digit. Do not count the left most digit as an exponent. If the number has 6 digits, the exponent should be 5.

If you are converting numbers from 0.999… and below:

First, identify the first digit after all of the zeroes. Copy it. Then, write a decimal point after the first digit that you copied. Then, write all of the remaining digits. Do not copy zeroes. You only need to copy zeroes if the last zero is followed by a decimal point, or if the last zero is followed by a whole number. Then, write “x”, followed by the number “10” Then, write the negative exponent after 10. The exponent is equal to all of the digits from the right of the decimal to the first digit after the last zero. If you have 0.000153, the exponent should be -4.

Learning Task 2: Writing Numbers in Scientific Notation

Scientific notation refers to the standard way of writing large numbers and very small numbers to make them easier to compare and use in computation. Below are the scientific notations of the given numbers:

2.75 × 10⁵6.3 × 10⁻⁴9 × 10³8 × 10²3.8 × 10⁻⁴

How to Write Numbers in Scientific Notation

Writing numbers in scientific notation follows the form of n × 10ᵃ. n represents the number between 1 and 10. Then, a represents an integer. These are the rules to remember on scientific notation.

Move the decimal point of the number until it becomes a number between 1 and 10.

The exponent or a is the number of places the decimal point was moved. It can be positive or negative depending whether ypu move the decimal point to the left or to the right.

The exponent is negative if you move the decimal point to the right. The exponent is positive if you move to the left.
