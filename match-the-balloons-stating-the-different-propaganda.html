20. Is the mass of the deflated balloon different from the mass of inflated balloon


Yes,slighly. To make it simple, assume that air is really just nitrogen, and that it behaves as an ideal gas. Molar mass of molecular nitrogen is 28 g/mol.

Assume that the inflated balloon is basically a sphere and the radius is 10 cm. Volume inside the balloon is therefore (4/3) x pi. x r^3 = 4189 cubic centimeters or 4.189 litre.

We know that at 25°C and 1 atm, a mole of ideal gas occupies 25.4 litres, so the balloon contains 4.189 / 25.4 = 0.16 moles of N2. This weights 28 x 0.16 = 4.5 g.

So the inflated balloon should weigh 4.5 g more than the deflated one.

What State of Matter Is Air?

When you look around, everything you see is matter. Everything takes up

space and has mass. You can sit in a chair. It is hard and always the same.

It has mass and takes up space. You can fill a sink with water, and when

you spill water on the floor, it forms a puddle. Whether in the sink or on the

floor, the water has mass and takes up space. The air you breathe is also

matter. But air is a very different type of matter than a chair or water. You

cannot sit on air, and you cannot see it. It does not form puddles on the

floor. Chairs, water in a puddle, and air are examples of different states of

matter. These states differ in very specific ways.

Two characteristics of matter—volume and shape—can help you determine

its state. Volume and shape are different for each state of matter. You

may already have some ideas about the volume and shape of different

states of matter. In this section, you will investigate these properties of

states of matter.
