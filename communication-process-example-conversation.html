5. Give examples of conversation wherein communication process is evident.The task is to write a script that obviously reveals the process of communication. Usethe format below.Senderencodingmessagechannelreceiverdiscording noise​


1. Sender:

Sender composes and sends message. This message is his. Communication begins when the sender formulates his message. First, he organizes thoughts so they're understandable. 'then, the teacher's lecture conveys the message.

2. Message:

The message is the sender's message. He can communicate verbally or nonverbally (through gestures or body language). No of the format. The message must be clear.

3. Encoding:

Once the sender knows the message, he chooses the code to use. Encoding gives meaning to a message or turns concepts into understandable codes. Encoding converts a message into readable words, symbols, or movements. All three are possible.

4. Channel :

Through the channel, sender-to-recipient messages travel. The phone, mail, Internet, radio, TV, and newspaper are all media. To communicate effectively, use the best route. For example, complex technical diagrams can't be communicated via phone.

Express mail is great. Consider the receiver's needs while choosing a channel. If the recipient can't read, the mail is useless. You can't choose a telephone if the recipient doesn't have a phone. Management must examine if the input is needed when choosing a channel.

5. Receiver:

The addressee receives the message. In a phone call, the sender can communicate with one receiver; in group discussions, seminars, and conferences, there may be multiple. The message must be designed, encoded, and transmitted clearly. Avoid jargon and elaborate symbols. The receiver is an observer, reader, or listener, depending on the channel.

6. Decoding:

Decoding interprets a message. The recipient interprets the symbols as best he can. Effective communication means the recipient understands the message. The recipient must understand the sender's codes and symbols.

7. Noise:

It shows how disruptive communication is. It affects message clarity and efficiency. The addressee may misinterpret the message. Common noise sources that reduce message quality include speaking close to a noisy machine, an emergency phone call, or the sender or recipient's discomfort.

What is communication?

Communication is typically defined as the exchange of information. The phrase can either be used to indicate the transmission of a single message or a specific area of research into such transmissions. There is considerable disagreement on its exact definition.

Communication, which might take the form of talking, writing, listening, or reading, includes giving, receiving, and exchanging information. Effective communicators listen carefully to others' viewpo1nts, speak or write clearly, and respect others' perspectives. The act of communicating involves giving and receiving signals via both verbal and nonverbal ways. The aim of communication is to foster understanding between two or more parties by the two-way exchange of information in the form of ideas, thoughts, and opinions.

Seven Elements of An Effective Communication

SENDER - The individual who is attempting to send a message is known as the sender.

RECEIVER - The message must be understood by the recipient, who must then convert the words into thoughts, analyze the thoughts, and choose how to respond to the sender.

MESSAGE - Congruency, or agreement, between the words, meaning, and emotion constitutes a successful message. Congruency requires careful attention to both vocal inflection and body expression.

CHANNEL - The channel is the medium the sender uses to communicate with the recipient. This could be done verbally, in writing, via text’ message, over the phone, via email, or by a third party.

NOISE - Interference that occurs during communication is referred to as noise. Noise has the potential to divert both the emitter and the receiver. Both internal (thoughts, emotions, etc.) and exterior sources can produce noise (radios, other conversations, etc.).

FEEDBACK - Feedback is the process of figuring out whether the message has been understood correctly. The sender or the receiver may start this.

CONTEXT - Another approach to consider the situation is through context.
