6. What are the observation that led to the modern atomic theory?​


Answer:

Long before the end of the 19th century, it was well known that applying a high voltage to a gas contained at low pressure in a sealed tube (called a gas discharge tube) caused electricity to flow through the gas, which then emitted light. Researchers trying to understand this phenomenon found that an unusual form of energy was also emitted from the cathode, or negatively charged electrode; this form of energy was called a cathode ray.

27. Point out the main ideas in the discovery of the structure of the atom and its subatomic particles, describe the nuclear model of the atom and the location of its major components 9protons, neutrons, and electrons0, explain how the concept of atomic number led to the synthesis of new elements in the laboratory.


Answer:

The smallest piece of an element that maintains the identity of that element is called an atom. Individual atoms are extremely small. It would take about fifty million atoms in a row to make a line that is 1 cm long. The period at the end of a printed sentence has several million atoms in it. Atoms are so small that it is difficult to believe that all matter is made from atoms—but it is.

The concept that atoms play a fundamental role in chemistry is formalized by the modern atomic theory, first stated by John Dalton, an English scientist, in 1808. It consists of three parts:

1.All matter is composed of atoms

2.Atoms of the same element are the same; atoms of different elements are different.

3.Atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds.

30. in 1897 Thomson discovered the electron and then went on to propose a model for the structure of the atom his work also led to the invention of the mass spectrograph explained?​


JJ Thomson's discovery of the electron in 1897 was a major breakthrough in the understanding of the atomic structure. He discovered that cathode rays, which were previously thought to be a type of wave, were actually made up of tiny, negatively charged particles called electrons. This led him to propose a new model for the structure of the atom, known as the plum pudding model, which suggested that the atom was composed of a positively charged substance with electrons embedded in it.

Thomson's work also led to the invention of the mass spectrograph, which is a device used to analyze the mass and abundance of different isotopes in a sample. The mass spectrograph works by ionizing the atoms in the sample, then separating them according to their mass-to-charge ratio, and finally detecting the resulting ion beams.

The invention of the mass spectrograph was significant because it allowed scientists to study the properties of atoms and molecules in more detail, including their mass, composition, and isotopic abundance. This, in turn, led to the development of new fields of study, such as nuclear physics and isotopic geochemistry, and has had important practical applications in areas such as medical research and environmental monitoring.
