Concrete, mortar, road stone, or asphalt (drainage courses), as well as construction fill and railroad ballast, all employ aggregates. Sand and gravel and crushed rock (including limestone, igneous rock, and sandstone) are the two main categories of natural aggregate.

Typical building minerals are aggregates (sand, gravel,  natural quarries), various clay bricks, gypsum, and natural ornamental or natural stone. Demand for construction minerals is generally high.

The largest component of building minerals and the bulkiest material extracted from the British landmass is 'aggregate'. The term is a term used to describe granular or particulate materials suitable for use alone or with binders such as cement, lime, and bitumen (in construction). Aggregate is used in concrete, mortar, crushed stone, asphalt (drainage layer), or for building filling and track ballast. In addition to sand and gravel collected from land, a significant amount is produced by dredging at sea. Other minerals used in the construction industry include clay, chalk, limestone, dolomite, brick loam, gypsum, slate, and building stone.

By varying the nature and proportions of its components, it is possible to obtain properties suitable for different applications. As a result, roads, bridges, tunnels, drainage pipes, retaining walls, soundproof walls, etc. can be made of concrete, and road infrastructure and towns and villages will continue to develop.  Concrete can be produced in on-site concrete plants (to produce what is known as ready-mixed concrete that is brought to the site by mixing trucks) or in precast plants that produce concrete elements such as pavements, slabs, beams, pipes, building components, etc.

Concrete is either unenforced, reinforced, prestressed, or fiber reinforced. It may be gray, white, or colored. Different surface treatments with different compositions and techniques can be used to give different surface appearances to concrete  (deactivated concrete, printed concrete, bush hammered concrete, shot peeved concrete, and abrasive concrete).

23. pencil what type of minerals are present ​


PENCIL

The majority of pencil cores are produced from a mixture of graphite powder and clay binder. Graphite pencils, sometimes referred to as "lead pencils," typically leave behind grey or black traces that may be quickly wiped but are otherwise resistant to moisture, the majority of chemicals, ultraviolet light, and aging from the environment. For drawing and sketching, other types of pencil cores, such those made of charcoal, are typically utilized.

Further explanation

A pencil is a writing or drawing tool with a solid pigment core enclosed in a shell for protection that lessens the chance of core breakage and prevents user hand marking. By physically abrading a surface, pencils leave a path of solid core material that sticks to a piece of paper or other surface. They are different from pens, which apply liquid or gel ink to the surface being marked.

However, colored pencils are often thought of as art materials, especially those with cores made from wax-based binders that tend to smear when erasers are used. Teachers and editors will occasionally use colored pencils to revise submitted texts. Grease pencils may leave stains on smooth surfaces like glass or porcelain due to their soft, oily substance.

The most typical pencil casing is thin wood, which is permanently bonded to the core and has a section that is often hexagonal but can occasionally be cylindrical or triangular. Other materials, such paper or plastic, can be used for casings. The pencil's case must be removed in order to reveal the core's sharp working end before it can be used.

In contrast to traditional pencils, mechanical pencils feature more sophisticated casings that accommodate independent, movable pigment cores that may be extended or retracted as needed, often through the tip of the casing. When the prior cores are used up, these casings may be reloaded with fresh ones, often made of graphite.
