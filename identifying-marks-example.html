PANDANGGUHAN

Pandanggo is the basis of another Philippine folk dance, Carinosa. Many people still dance pandanggo, but it is largely used in religious ceremonies and processions, such as the Pandangguhan sa Pasig during the St. Martha procession and the Sayaw sa Obando, which has a pandanggo for childless couples. Pandanggo sa Ilaw is a traditional Philippine folk dance that is said to have originated in Mindoro. By balancing illuminated cups, this light dance resembles fireflies in the night. Antonio Buenaventura from Bulacan, Philippines, composed the music, while Levi Cererio wrote the lyrics.

Vivace

Vivace is an Italian word that means "lively" or "vivid." The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) is used to pronounce it. The term vivace is an Italian musical term that refers to a lively movement.

Moderato

Moderato, like andante ("at a walking tempo"), is a musical phrase that might be confusing. It literally means "moderately," but what does that really mean. It's a tempo marking that's slower than allegretto but faster than andante at its most basic level.

Allegro

Allegro (Italian for "joyous, lively") is commonly understood to signify "rapid," though not as quickly as vivace or presto. Allegretto is a diminutive of allegro, meaning slightly slower. Allegro is a fast pace that ranges from 120 to 168 beats per minute. Allegro or 120bpm are options for the composer. It's worth noting that I typed "between 120 and 168 beats per minute," because tempo marks frequently include a range.

Presto

Presto translates to "suddenly" or "very quickly." Magicians enjoy yelling "presto!" prior to a trick To perform something presto in music means to play it at an extremely rapid speed. Presto, which means "quickly" in Italian, is the second-fastest pace at which music can be played (after prestissimo).

Ritardando

Ritardando (abbreviated – rit.) is an Italian word that means "to slow down and then stop," but rallentando normally refers to a gradual slowing down of the pace, whereas ritardando can apply to either a gradual or rapid slowdown, followed by a complete halt.
