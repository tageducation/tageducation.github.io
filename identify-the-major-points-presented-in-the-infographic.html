CYBERCRIME LAW

What is Cybercrime?

Any criminal activity committed using a network, technology, or the internet is considered cybercrime. The great majority of cybercrimes are undertaken for financial gain, however, some are meant to damage the victim. Both people and businesses are targets. People are frequently involved in larger attacks where hackers attempt to infect computers with malware for financial gain. On the other hand, business assaults are typically one-off incidents. Businesses are also much more likely to be the target of hacktivist protests, which are a kind of cybercrime in and of itself.

The term ""cybercrime,"" often known as ""computer crime,"" refers to the use of a computer to advance criminal activities including fraud,  infringements on intellectual property, identity theft, or invasions of privacy. Cybercrime differs from ordinary criminal offenses in that it involves the use of a computer to carry out the offense. Attacks against private information belonging to people, organizations, or governments make up the majority of cybercrime.

What is Cybercrime Law?

Cybercrime law covers legislation governing offenses involving computers, the internet, information, communications, and technology. The internet and the digital economy offer great opportunities, but they also facilitate criminal activities. Laws that define cybercrimes' offenses and punishments are known as cybercrime laws. Regulations to lower risk and/or mitigate the harm done to people, organizations, and infrastructure should a cybercrime occur are also provided by cybercrime law. These regulations cover how people should use the Internet, computers, and other related digital technologies as well as how the public, government, and private organizations should behave. They also cover rules of evidence and criminal procedure as well as other criminal justice matters in cyberspace. Therefore, substantive, procedural, and preventive laws are all included in cybercrime law.
