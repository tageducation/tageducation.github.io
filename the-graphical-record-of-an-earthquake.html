7. The graphical record of an earthquake​


The Graphical Record of An Earthquake

A seismograph is a major earthquake measuring instrument. A seismograph consists of a ballast hanger and a pencil-like point. Thus, the strength of the vibration and the direction of the earthquake can be known through the description of the movement of the earth or ground caused by seismic waves.

Seismic waves that occur during an earthquake are depicted as wavy lines on a seismogram. Seismologists can calculate the magnitude of the earthquake from these lines.

In the past, seismographs could only detect horizontal movements, but along with the development of technology, seismographs have been able to record vertical and lateral movements. Seismographs use two motions, namely mechanical and electromagnetic seismographers. Two types of movement can detect both vertical and horizontal movements depending on the pendular used, whether vertical or horizontal.

Some of the seismographs that have been made include:

Wiechert mechanical seismographMilne-Shaw . optical mechanical seismographTorsion/torque seismographMoving coil seismographSeismograph press and ewingBenioff reluctance seismographBenioff electrostatic seismographBenioff strain seismograph

Seismographs use two different classifications in measuring seismic waves generated during an earthquake, namely the magnitude of the earthquake and the intensity of the earthquake. The two measurement classifications use different measurement scales, including:

1. The Richter scale is used to describe the magnitude of an earthquake

The Richter scale was developed by an American geophysics named Charles Francis Richter (1900-1985) together with Beno Gutenberg (1889-1960). The Richter scale is still widely used in general standards for earthquake strength scales around the world.

2. The Mercalli scale is used to indicate the intensity of an earthquake, or the effect an earthquake has on land, buildings, and people.

The Mercalli scale was developed by a volcanologist from Italy named Giuseppe Mercalli and classifies the intensity scale of the influence of earthquakes on humans, buildings, and nature (soil). Giuseppe Mercalli determined the scale based on interviews with victims, so it is very subjective. Then, in 1931 an American scientist modified the Mercalli Scale which until now is widely used for areas that frequently occur earthquakes.
