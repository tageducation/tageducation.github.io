Yes, Since muscles are forced to work harder than they are used to, they experience one type of pain - a slow, burning ache in the muscles that is common and not cause for concern. This "natural" burning shows that you've reached the anaerobic threshold, which means you've reached the end of your stamina. The discomfort is said to be caused by lactic acid accumulation in your muscles, which happens when your muscles do not get enough oxygen (anaerobic means "in the absence of oxygen").

This burning sensation does not indicate an injury, but rather that you have reached your fitness limit. (Some world-class athletes and elite athletes claim that a massage after stretching extracts lactic acid from their muscles until they have cooled off, allowing them to compete at their best the next day.) The more you exercise, the greater your anaerobic threshold (limit of endurance), and you will soon be able to exercise for longer stretches of time and at a more intense pace.  Feeling sore after an activity does not usually indicate that something is wrong, particularly if it happens the next day. Unless you've really been injured, the pain can subside after a day or two, gradually creating lean muscle in place of flab. That is why you should space your exercises and change your tasks so that different muscle groups are exercised on different days, with a rest day in between.

What is Pain?

Pain is the body's way of reminding you that you have injured it. This is a positive thing, particularly if you are hurt. It may also aid in the diagnosis of medical issues. Pain will last for a long time until it is no longer required. Amputees experience phantom pain in their missing legs or limbs. There are several types of pain, and defining the form will help in diagnosis: recurring, continuous, steady, knife-like, radiating, sharp, dull. Analgesics are medications that relieve discomfort. Anesthetics are those that numb all feelings.
