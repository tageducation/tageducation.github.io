1. Reliving the Past and Embracing the Future through Philippine Literature Essay​


Answer:

The literature of the Philippines is rich and diverse, reflecting the country's history, culture, and values. Through literature, we are able to relive the past and understand the experiences of our ancestors. We are also able to embrace the future by learning from the lessons of the past and using them to shape our present and future.

One of the most notable periods in Philippine literature is the colonial period, when the country was under Spanish rule. During this time, literature was used as a means of resistance and protest against colonial oppression. Writers such as Jose Rizal, Graciano Lopez Jaena, and Marcelo H. del Pilar used their works to advocate for reform and independence. Their writing inspired a national consciousness and played a significant role in the country's eventual independence.

Today, Philippine literature continues to evolve and reflect the changing times. It reflects the struggles and triumphs of the Filipino people, as well as their hopes and dreams for the future. Through literature, we are able to connect with our past and move forward towards a better future. By reading and engaging with Philippine literature, we are able to gain a deeper understanding of our country's history and culture and use it to shape a bright and prosperous future.

Past and Present Philippines Literature

Philippine literature is the literature linked with the Philippines from prehistory to the present, including its colonial legacy. Actually, pre-Hispanic Philippine literature consisted of epics passed down orally from generation to generation.

Filipino literature has been impacted by Spanish colonialism and, later, by the Americans' long-term presence. During the Spanish era, Filipinos began to compose "long poetry" that combined Catholicism and nationalism.

Unique to the Philippines is the presence of significant works in multiple languages. These could be divided into four categories: Spanish, Tagalog, English, and other Philippine languages' literature. It may appear that these distinct collections of work only correspond to an era of colonial dominance, but this is deceptive.

Dr. Jose Rizal's Noli Me Tangere (Touch Me Not) is the most controversial and well-known work of literature in the Philippines. The novel has had such an impact on the country that it is incorporated into the current curriculum for high school students in the Philippines.

Literature teaches us language and the power of communication, an essential ability in the twenty-first century. Literature teaches us humanity — to be sensitive and kind toward others.

Reading Filipino literature increases cultural awareness and knowledge, fostering a better appreciation of the culture. The more they appreciate their own culture, the simpler it will be for them to establish their citizenship as Filipinos.

English Month: Reliving the Past and Embracing the Future through Philippine Literature.

The literature associated with the Philippines from prehistory to the present, including its colonial heritage, is known as Philippine literature. Actually, oral epics that were passed down from generation to generation made up pre-Hispanic Philippine literature.

Timeline of how Philippine Literature evolved

The Pre-Colonial Era (BC to 1564)

Legends, Crude on ideology and phraseology, Riddle, Proverbs, Tanaga Fables, Fantastic stories, Epics, Folk Song, Folk Tales, Myths (explain how the world was created, how certain animals possess certain characteristics, why some places have waterfalls, volcanoes, mountains, flora or fauna), Crude on ideology and phraseology

Period Of Spanish Colonization (1565 – 1863)

It is divided into two categories: religious and secular.Spanish was made available as a language of communication.Pasyon, Senakulo, Secular, Awit, Korido Prose Narratives, Dialogo are examples of religious literature.

The Nationalistic/Propaganda and Revolutionary Era (1864 – 1896)

Instilled nationalistic values in FilipinosThe language was changed to Tagalog from Spanish.Addressed the populace rather than the "intellectsia"Political essays, Diariong, Noli Me Tangere, and El Filibusterismo are examples of revolutionary literature.

American Colonial Era

Apprenticeship PeriodFilipino Writers imitated English and American modelsPoems written were amateurish and mushy, which phrasing and diction is awkward and artificialShort Stories: The Key Footnote to Youth, Childe ofPeriod of Emergence (1920-1930): Highly influenced by Western literary trends like Romanticism and Realism

Opportunity in Japanese (1942 - 1960)

A Long War (1942-1944)As Tagalog authors departed from the Balagtas tradition and wrote in straightforward prose and free verse, fiction took precedence over poetry.Filipino writers improved their driving abilities while studying English.

Modern/Contemporary Period (1960 – Present)

Martial Law suppressed and restricted human rights, such as press freedomDespite severe censorship, writers resorted to symbolism and allegory to emphasize their points.Groups like PETA (Philippine Educational Theater Association) and UP Theater employed theater as a means of protest.Writers have continued to be dynamic and innovative since the 1980s.
