3. In what direction did the tropical cyclones move?.​


Answer:

Tropical cyclones, also known as typhoons or hurricanes, are large, low-pressure systems that form over tropical or subtropical waters. They are characterized by strong winds, heavy rain, and often cause significant damage when they make landfall.

Tropical cyclones typically move in a generally westward direction, driven by the easterly trade winds in the tropics. However, their exact path and speed can vary widely, depending on a number of factors such as the strength and direction of upper-level winds, the presence of other weather systems, and the topography of the land over which they pass.

In some cases, tropical cyclones can move in a more northwesterly or southwesterly direction, depending on the steering forces acting on them. They can also become stationary or even loop around and move in a different direction, although this is less common.

Explanation:

The impact of tropical cyclones that are felt directly by the area they pass are in the form of high waves, storm surges, namely rising sea levels such as high tides that come suddenly, heavy rains and strong winds. Tropical cyclones are storms of great strength. The average radius of a tropical cyclone is 150 to 200 km. Tropical cyclones form over vast oceans which generally have a warm sea surface temperature of more than 26.5°C. Strong winds swirling near its center have wind speeds of over 63 km/h.

Technically, a tropical cyclone is defined as a synoptic scale non-frontal low pressure system that grows over warm waters with areas of convective cloud and maximum wind speeds of at least 34 knots in more than half of the area surrounding its center, and lasting at least six hours.

Sometimes at the center of a tropical cyclone, an area with relatively low wind speeds and no clouds is formed, known as the eye of the cyclone. The eye diameter of a cyclone varies from 10 to 100 km. The eye of the cyclone is surrounded by an eye wall, which is a ring-shaped area that can reach a thickness of 16 km, which is the area where there are the highest wind speeds and the greatest rainfall. The average lifespan of a tropical cyclone is from 3 to 18 days. Because tropical cyclone energy is obtained from warm oceans, tropical cyclones will weaken or become extinct when they move and enter cold water areas or enter land.
