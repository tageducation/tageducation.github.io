18. where can you situate yourself a spiritual or a religious person?​


Answer:

Both are essential.

The spiritual is the heart of religion, the life within. Atheists and other outsiders to a religious tradition look from outside, see the bare rituals, and find it foolish. This is because they don't see the internal experience, the experience of being part of the community, and the internal spirtitual experience. The letter of the law looks flat and cold and dead without a comprehension of the spirit running through it. And to a certain extent, they are justified in this, because we see so many religious people these days for whom the outside is all there is, the mere repetition of ritual without feeling, the rigid application of the law without reflection or thinking. This is like gnawing on the stripped away rind of what was once a delicious melon.

Religion is in a sense a scaffold, a trail of breadcrumbs, a thread you hold onto to find your way out of a maze. It is a set of time proven exercises to help guide you, given the right mindset and effort on your part, to an experiential spiritual reality. It is a means to the end of kindling a guiding light within.

Religions are the remnants of the original transcendant experience of a founding figure or figures. The founders of religion are shining, luminous figures that managed, through striving, luck, and Providence, to connect into the mainline. They shone like stars against the drudgery of their contemporary reality. Islam, for example (and Judaism too, I think), speaks of there having been somewhere over 100 000 prophets and messengers. According to estimates of the number of humans that have ever lived, about 100 billion, this makes them people that were literally one in a million. These figures found a program that worked for them. People saw them, saw their quality, and were drawn to them, and to the God they spoke of, whose light shone through them like fire in a lamp. People see the spiritual energy within these founders and feel it flow out from them. When these figures pass on, they live on, first in the memories of those who met them, and in the example of the best of those inspired by meeting the founder. These people shine with a secondary light of sorts, a reflection of the light from that original lamp.

Eventually, even the people that met and knew the founder pass on. At that point all you are left with is the written descriptions of the founder and the early community. History. Biography. The rich living sense of context grows fainter, and takes effort to uncover. A class of clergy appears to study and help bring that spiritual reality to life in a later time and a different place. You will have a few strains of the most dedicated followers who transmitted the deepest, insider aspects of the tradition, passing the time-dimmed flame from hand to hand down through the centuries like some sacred family heirloom.

Religion only works when it fulfills that end of getting the warm spiritual fire going within. Otherwise you're just furiously rubbing sticks together in the dark. It's the shell without the substance. This is what is so frightening and incomprehensible about the likes of ISIS. They fervently follow the letter (though even here they fail through literalism and lack of contextual understanding), but behind the eyes, the spirit is dead as doornails. But they pursue it with full energy nonetheless. "The best lack all conviction, while the worst are full of passionate intensity..."

On the other hand, spirituality on its own, without religion, while good, and probably a few steps better than religion without spirituality, is (for most) incomplete and directionless. The religious tradition provides structure and discipline that most people find useful. It gives a program that has been time tested in the field in a wide swath of different circumstances, for different types of people. That structure helps to chart a known, reliable path to spirituality for the masses, linked into the continuity of a tradition.
